# Unique Findings: Cross-Review Reconciliation

This document identifies findings raised by only one or two of the three LLM reviewers (Claude, GPT, Gemini) of Scotland's AI Strategy 2026-2031. For each finding, it notes which reviewer(s) raised it, which missed it, and whether the omission matters.

---

## 1. Findings Unique to Claude Only

### 1.1 Action repetition inflates apparent volume

**Finding:** Several actions appear multiple times across different sections of the strategy (e.g., the public services innovation programme appears in the ten key actions, Layer 4, and Layer 7; the Expert Advisory Board appears in key actions and Layer 1). This creates an impression of more activity than the strategy actually proposes.

**Evidence cited:** Claude tracked the public services innovation programme across sections 06, 12, and 15, and the Expert Advisory Board across sections 06 and 09, providing a specific count of repeated actions.

**Missed by:** GPT and Gemini.

**Does the omission matter?** Yes. This is a structural observation about the strategy's integrity as a delivery document. If three of ten headline actions are recycled into layer-level actions without acknowledgment, the total volume of new commitments is overstated. Neither GPT nor Gemini flagged this pattern despite reviewing the same sections. GPT came closest by noting that many actions "create new bodies, pilots, and programmes" but did not identify the specific duplication. This is a genuine gap in the other two reviews.

---

### 1.2 Acquisition risk for Scottish AI companies

**Finding:** Successful Scottish AI companies are frequently acquired by larger international firms and relocated. This is arguably the primary threat to building a sustainable AI ecosystem, and the strategy does not address it.

**Evidence cited:** Raised in the Layer 3 (Companies and Products) review and the summary's "Key Gaps" table.

**Missed by:** GPT and Gemini.

**Does the omission matter?** Yes. GPT noted the gap between ambition and structural constraints on scaling, and Gemini noted the difficulty of attracting VC capital to Scotland versus London or Silicon Valley, but neither identified the specific and well-documented pattern of successful Scottish tech companies being acquired and their headquarters relocated. This is a distinct, empirically observable risk that goes beyond generic scaling difficulty. Both GPT and Gemini should have caught it.

---

### 1.3 The "296 AI companies" figure needs scrutiny

**Finding:** The count of 296 AI-focused companies comes from a non-public report commissioned by Scottish Enterprise. The definition spans "start-ups, scale-ups, research institutions, innovation centres and specialist technical consultancies" -- a very broad definition that inflates the number.

**Evidence cited:** Claude traced the figure to endnote 12 and examined the methodology implied by the source description.

**Missed by:** GPT and Gemini.

**Does the omission matter?** Yes. Gemini cited the 296 figure uncritically as evidence of a "dynamic business ecosystem." GPT mentioned it but did not question the methodology. Source scrutiny is a core part of strategy review; if the headline company count is inflated by definitional breadth and based on an unpublished source, that undermines the Foundations section's credibility. Claude's source-level scrutiny was a genuine value-add.

---

### 1.4 The Isle of Eigg case study is mismatched to the Data Centres section

**Finding:** The case study in the Data Centres and Infrastructure section (Layer 5) describes a 100-person community generating 280 kW of renewable power. A single hyperscale data centre requires 100-500+ MW. The case study demonstrates community-led renewables, not AI infrastructure capability, and feels included to celebrate a Scottish success story rather than to illustrate the section's argument.

**Evidence cited:** Claude compared the power output of Eigg's community grid (280 kW) against hyperscale data centre requirements (100-500+ MW), a difference of roughly three orders of magnitude.

**Missed by:** GPT and Gemini.

**Does the omission matter?** Moderately. GPT mentioned the Eigg case study without questioning its relevance. Gemini did not discuss case study fitness at all. Claude's observation is valid -- the mismatch between a community micro-grid and hyperscale AI infrastructure is stark -- but it is an editorial quality point rather than a policy gap. Still, it reveals a pattern of the strategy deploying feel-good examples rather than analytically relevant ones.

---

### 1.5 Gigged.AI appears twice in the strategy

**Finding:** The same company (Gigged.AI) is quoted in the Introduction (section 02) and used as the case study in Layer 3 (Companies and Products). This suggests a thin pool of examples and, in the case of Layer 3, the company demonstrates enterprise hiring solutions rather than sovereign AI capability -- the section's central argument.

**Evidence cited:** Claude cross-referenced the company name across sections 02 and 11.

**Missed by:** GPT and Gemini.

**Does the omission matter?** Mildly. It is a quality observation about the strategy's evidence base. GPT reviewed both sections without noting the repetition. The deeper point -- that the case study does not illustrate the section's sovereignty argument -- is more significant than the repetition itself.

---

### 1.6 Intellectual property is mentioned once and never addressed

**Finding:** IP is referenced in the opening of the Regulation section but is never substantively addressed. AI-generated content, model training on copyrighted data, and IP ownership are live issues with significant implications for Scotland's creative industries and research base.

**Evidence cited:** Claude's review of section 16 (Regulation) and the summary's gap table.

**Missed by:** GPT and Gemini.

**Does the omission matter?** Yes. IP in AI is one of the most contested legal and economic issues globally. The strategy names creative industries as a priority sector and mentions IP once, then drops it. GPT's regulation review discussed practical agency and enforcement but did not flag IP. Gemini's regulation review focused on constitutional constraints but also missed IP entirely. This is a genuine gap in all three reviews, though only Claude caught it.

---

### 1.7 No mention of accessibility (disabled users, non-English speakers, those without internet)

**Finding:** For a strategy emphasising inclusive AI literacy, there is no mention of accessibility for disabled users, non-English speakers (including Gaelic speakers in a Scottish context), or those without internet access.

**Evidence cited:** Claude's review of Layer 1 (Users) and the summary's gap table.

**Missed by:** GPT and Gemini.

**Does the omission matter?** Yes. GPT noted the section was "light on redress" and Gemini noted vagueness in measurability, but neither identified the specific absence of accessibility considerations. In a strategy that repeatedly uses the word "inclusive," the failure to mention disability, language barriers, or digital exclusion is a significant oversight. Both other reviewers should have caught this.

---

### 1.8 No right to challenge AI decisions

**Finding:** The strategy says citizens should understand "what safeguards apply" but describes no complaints process, right to appeal, or requirement for a human alternative when AI is involved in public service decisions.

**Evidence cited:** Claude's review of Layer 1 (Users) and the summary's gap table.

**Missed by:** Gemini. GPT partially caught it.

**Does the omission matter?** Yes. GPT noted that the Users section was "light on redress" and that the strategy "does not explain what citizens can do if they are harmed by an AI-enabled decision." However, GPT framed this as a general observation, while Claude identified the specific missing mechanisms (complaints process, right to appeal, human alternative). Gemini missed the point entirely, focusing on the positive framing of citizen engagement. The specificity of Claude's finding adds value.

### 1.9 Education gap -- no action on schools, colleges, or curricula

**Finding:** Despite "prepared young people" being a stated 2031 outcome, none of the ten key actions and none of the Layer 1 or Layer 2 actions address AI in schools, colleges, or university curricula. The outcome has no delivery mechanism.

**Evidence cited:** Claude cross-referenced the outcome statement in section 05 against actions in sections 06, 09, and 10, finding no corresponding delivery action.

**Missed by:** Gemini. GPT partially caught it.

**Does the omission matter?** Yes. GPT noted that skills and workforce material was more developed than other parts but did not specifically flag the absence of education-sector actions. Gemini did not raise this at all. Claude's point is sharp: a named outcome with zero corresponding actions is a structural failure in the strategy's logic. This should have been caught by all three reviewers.

**Evidence cited:** Claude's review of the Foundations section (section 03).

**Missed by:** GPT and Gemini.

**Does the omission matter?** Moderately. Gemini cited the figure uncritically as evidence of "accelerating investment." GPT noted the figure without interrogating it. The point is valid -- extraordinary claims require context -- but it is possible that further detail exists outside the strategy document itself.

### 1.11 Research Data Scotland is not mentioned in the Data layer

**Evidence cited:** Claude's review of section 15 (Layer 7 - Data).

**Missed by:** GPT and Gemini.

**Does the omission matter?** Moderately. This is a specific institutional knowledge point. The strategy proposes a "data matchmaking pilot" when an existing body already performs a related function. The omission suggests either a gap in the strategy's awareness of existing infrastructure or a deliberate choice not to reference it -- either way, worth flagging.

### 1.12 Private sector data is entirely absent from the Data layer

**Evidence cited:** Claude's review of section 15 (Layer 7 - Data).

**Missed by:** GPT and Gemini.

Given the reserved/devolved dynamic that all three reviewers acknowledge elsewhere, this is a notable gap.

### 1.14 Creative industries -- only sector where risks are acknowledged in Foundations

**Evidence cited:** Claude's review of section 03 (Foundations).

## 2. Findings Unique to GPT Only

### 2.1 The strategy assumes coordination and convening will overcome structural barriers

**Finding:** The strategy often implies that partnership-building, ecosystem coordination, and stakeholder engagement will be sufficient to address deep structural problems, without showing what additional powers, funding, or institutional change will actually be required.

**Evidence cited:** GPT's summary and multiple section reviews (particularly AI Scotland, Actions, Innovation & R&D).

**Missed by:** Claude and Gemini.

**Does the omission matter?** Moderately. Claude identified specific examples of this pattern (machinery without results, panels without deliverables) but framed them as measurability and specificity failures rather than as a systemic assumption about what coordination can achieve. Gemini was generally more positive about the coordination model. GPT's framing as a distinct strategic assumption -- that convening equals delivering -- is a valid meta-observation that the other two reviews approached from different angles but did not articulate as cleanly.

---

### 2.2 The glossary leans toward infrastructure and business language, leaving governance and public-interest concepts underexplained

**Finding:** The glossary's definitions are weighted toward technical and commercial terms, while governance, ethics, and public-interest terms are less well covered.

**Evidence cited:** GPT's review of section 18 (Glossary).

**Missed by:** Claude and Gemini (partially).

**Does the omission matter?** Mildly. Claude identified specific missing terms (AI, AGI, Fair Work, AI Stack). Gemini identified the absence of ethical terms ("Trustworthy AI", "Responsible AI", "Fair Work"). GPT's observation is about the overall balance rather than specific omissions. All three noticed glossary gaps, but from different angles; GPT's framing as a systemic lean toward business language is a distinct observation.

---

### 2.3 The acknowledgments signal breadth of engagement but limited transparency on who shaped decisions

**Finding:** Many contributors are described in aggregate ("over 100 experts") rather than named, making it impossible to assess whose perspectives carried most weight in shaping the strategy.

**Evidence cited:** GPT's review of section 19 (Acknowledgments).

**Missed by:** Gemini. Claude partially caught it.

**Does the omission matter?** Moderately. Claude noted the narrow composition of the named advisory group and the absence of trade unions and public sector representatives. GPT made the additional point about transparency -- that aggregate descriptions obscure influence. Gemini read the acknowledgments positively, noting the presence of a Human Data Interaction expert as reinforcing ethical aims. GPT's transparency point is valid and distinct from Claude's composition point.

---

### 2.4 The strategy does not distinguish between what Scotland can control directly and what depends on UK-wide or international conditions

**Finding:** Across multiple sections, the strategy blurs the line between ambitions Scotland can deliver with devolved powers and those that require UK Government cooperation or international alignment.

**Evidence cited:** GPT's reviews of Case for Change, AI Action Plan, Infrastructure, Regulation, and summary.

**Missed by:** Gemini (partially). Claude partially caught it.

**Does the omission matter?** Partially. Claude identified this specifically in the Regulation and Infrastructure sections (grid connectivity as a reserved power, regulatory scope as a constitutional question). Gemini identified it as a headline "Constitutional and External Dependencies" risk in its summary. But GPT was the most consistent in raising this across multiple sections as a systemic pattern, not just an issue in one or two layers. GPT's cross-cutting framing adds value, though the underlying observation was not entirely missed by the others.

---

### 2.5 Procurement reform and market-shaping are underdeveloped

**Finding:** Public-sector demand is presented as a possible driver of innovation, but the strategy is thin on procurement reform and market-shaping detail that would actually channel public spending toward domestic AI products and services.

**Evidence cited:** GPT's reviews of Innovation & R&D (section 12) and the summary's "Research, innovation, and commercialisation" theme.

**Missed by:** Claude and Gemini.

**Does the omission matter?** Yes. Claude noted the commercialisation gap and the public services innovation programme but did not specifically identify procurement reform as a missing lever. Gemini noted public sector procurement as a "reference market" positively. GPT's point is that procurement is one of the few tools the Scottish Government directly controls that could shape the AI market, and the strategy barely addresses it. This is a genuine gap in the other two reviews.

## 3. Findings Unique to Gemini Only

### 3.1 The AI Stack borrows smartly from software engineering but may alienate non-technical audiences

**Finding:** While the "AI Stack" is resonant and effective for digital professionals, it may require careful government communication to ensure that politicians, community groups, and lay audiences do not find the structural terminology alienating.

**Evidence cited:** Gemini's review of section 08 (AI Action Plan).

**Missed by:** Claude and GPT.

**Does the omission matter?** Mildly. Claude critiqued the AI Stack as overselling its novelty ("radical" is overstated) and noted its structural inconsistencies (non-hierarchical claim contradicted by vertical layout). GPT noted it was not fully justified as a novel model. Neither raised the audience accessibility question. Gemini's point is valid for implementation -- if the strategy's central organising metaphor confuses its intended audience, communication will suffer -- but it is a presentation concern rather than a policy gap.

### 3.2 The ten specific 2027 actions provide an "excellent structural baseline for near-term accountability"

**Finding:** Gemini uniquely characterised the time-bound 2027 actions as a positive structural feature that contrasts with the vague 2031 outcomes, describing the specificity of the deadline as providing a "rigid timeline for strict governmental accountability."

**Evidence cited:** Gemini's summary and section 06 review.

**Missed by:** Not exactly missed -- Claude and GPT both noted the March 2027 deadline as welcome. But neither framed it as strongly positive. Claude and GPT both focused on what the actions lacked (targets, budgets, success criteria) rather than crediting the accountability mechanism of the deadline itself.

**Does the omission matter?** No -- this is a difference in evaluative emphasis rather than a missed finding. All three reviewers noted the 2027 deadline; they simply weighted it differently.

## 4. Findings Shared by Two Reviewers but Missed by the Third

### 4.1 Fair Work is rhetorical rather than operationally embedded

**Raised by:** Claude and GPT.
**Missed by:** Gemini.

**Finding:** Both Claude and GPT identified that despite Fair Work being positioned as central in the foreword, it is not embedded in governance structures, delivery mechanisms, or the composition of AI Scotland's consortium. Trade union representation is absent from AI Scotland's partners and the Expert Advisory Board's criteria.

**Does the omission matter?** Yes. Gemini mentioned Fair Work as a glossary gap and noted its absence from glossary definitions, but never identified the broader pattern of Fair Work being a rhetorical commitment without delivery mechanisms. This is a significant gap in Gemini's analysis.

### 4.2 Phases 2 and 3 of the strategy are empty

**Raised by:** Claude and GPT.
**Missed by:** Gemini.

**Finding:** The strategy is presented as a five-year plan (2026-2031) delivered in three phases, but only Phase 1 actions are defined. Phases 2 (2027) and 3 (2029) are blank. This means the strategy is effectively a 12-month action plan in a five-year wrapper.

**Does the omission matter?** Yes. Gemini described the phased approach as a positive feature ("builds a predictable rhythm for external auditors to assess longitudinal strategy execution") without noting that the later phases have no content. This is a straightforward factual observation that Gemini should have caught. It is one of the most significant weaknesses in the strategy's delivery framework.

## 5. Summary Assessment

### Pattern of strengths and weaknesses across reviewers

**Claude** was the most granular reviewer. Its distinctive strengths were: source scrutiny (questioning the 296 companies figure, tracing non-public reports), cross-referencing within the strategy (tracking action repetition, Fair Work references, case study reuse), and identifying specific missing mechanisms (right to appeal, accessibility, IP). Its main weakness: some findings are editorial quality points rather than policy-significant.

**GPT** was the most architecturally minded reviewer. Its distinctive strengths were: identifying systemic assumptions (coordination-as-delivery), framing the devolved/reserved power boundary as a cross-cutting constraint, and noting procurement reform as a missing lever. Its main weakness: it tended toward balanced, hedged language that sometimes softened the sharpness of its observations.

**Gemini** was the most sympathetic reviewer. Its distinctive strengths were: recognising the AI Stack's communication challenges for non-technical audiences, elevating institutional inertia as a cross-cutting feasibility risk, and identifying positive design choices (Net Zero alignment, existing consortium leverage). Its main weaknesses were significant: it failed to note the absence of budgets across the entire strategy, did not flag that Phases 2 and 3 are empty, missed the absence of trade unions from governance, and did not raise the lack of international benchmarks. Gemini's review was consistently more positive than the other two, which meant it missed several of the strategy's most fundamental structural weaknesses.

### Findings all three reviewers missed

No reviewer identified:
- The absence of a definition of "AI" in a document titled "AI Strategy" (Claude noted this in the glossary but none elevated it as a strategy-level finding)
- Whether the strategy's phased model has any mechanism for deciding what enters Phases 2 and 3 (criteria, governance, triggers)
- The absence of any mention of local government beyond data sharing -- councils are major service delivery bodies affected by AI but receive almost no attention
- Whether the "AI readiness tool" proposed for SMEs has any precedent or evidence base
