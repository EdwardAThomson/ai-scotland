# Disagreements Between Reviewers

Disagreements between independent reviewers are particularly valuable in a reconciliation exercise. Where three separate analyses converge, we can be reasonably confident in the finding. Where they diverge, the point of difference often reveals something important -- an ambiguity in the source material, a question of analytical judgement, or a genuine interpretive choice about what matters most. This document catalogues every substantive disagreement identified across the Claude, GPT, and Gemini reviews of Scotland's AI Strategy 2026-2031, drawing on all 21 section-level reviews and 3 summary documents.

---

## 1. Assessment of the Foundations Section: Promotional Catalogue vs. Well-Evidenced Base

**The disagreement:** Claude and GPT both characterise the Foundations section as one-sided promotional material that omits weaknesses. Gemini treats the same section as well-evidenced and clear.

- **Claude** (03-foundations.md): "The section reads more like an investment prospectus than a strategy foundation. It catalogues strengths effectively but does not analyse weaknesses, dependencies, or risks. A 'foundations' section that only lists assets gives an incomplete picture of the starting position." Claude specifically criticises the "296 AI companies" figure as inflated by a broad definition and flags the absence of any honest assessment of weaknesses.
- **GPT** (03-foundations.md): "It functions more as a promotional inventory than an assessment of foundations. It names assets and success stories, yet gives very little sense of structural weakness, dependency, or constraint." GPT calls the main omission "a serious account of what Scotland lacks."
- **Gemini** (03-foundations.md): "The document provides numerous concrete examples of projects, academic centres, and companies, making its assertions of a strong 'foundation' very clear and well-evidenced." Gemini rates the section positively on clarity and measurability, noting that specific investment values and gigawatt figures offer "baseline metrics that could be evaluated over time." Gemini does not flag any missing weaknesses.

**Assessment:** Claude and GPT are better supported here. A strategy foundations section that only catalogues strengths without acknowledging weaknesses is analytically incomplete by definition, regardless of how well-sourced the strength claims are. Gemini appears to have evaluated the section on its own terms (are the claims supported?) rather than asking whether the section serves its strategic function (does it give an honest baseline?). The absence of any mention of brain drain, venture capital gaps, or compute deficits is a genuine omission, not a matter of framing.

## 2. The AI Stack as a Framework: "Radical" Innovation vs. Borrowed Concept

**The disagreement:** The reviewers differ on whether the AI Stack is a genuinely novel and valuable policy contribution or a conventional model repackaged with promotional language.

- **Claude** (08-ai-action-plan.md): "Describing the AI Stack as 'radical' overstates its novelty. Layered models for technology ecosystems are well-established (the OSI model, technology stack diagrams, etc.). What is useful here is adapting the concept for policy purposes." Claude also criticises the claim that layers are "non-hierarchical" while being presented in a vertical stack as "contradictory."
- **GPT** (findings-summary.md): "The AI Stack provides a workable organising frame for the rest of the document, even if it is not fully justified as a novel policy model." In the section review: "Calling it a 'radical new approach' overstates how much explanatory work the section actually does."
- **Gemini** (summary.md): "The adoption of the 'AI Stack' is a highly resonant and logically robust framework... the strategy demonstrates a sophisticated structural understanding of modern technology ecosystems." In the section review: "The 'AI Stack' paradigm smartly borrows from software engineering principles" and "Placing 'Data' and 'Regulation' as encircling rings is conceptually stellar."

**Assessment:** This is a genuine difference in analytical standards. Claude and GPT evaluate the novelty claim critically and find it overstated. Gemini accepts the framework at closer to face value, praising its logic and resonance. The underlying question -- is the AI Stack useful? -- gets a "yes, but don't oversell it" from Claude and GPT, and an enthusiastic "yes" from Gemini. Claude and GPT are more rigorous on this point; the OSI model comparison is apt, and describing a layered technology framework as "radical" is a promotional claim that a critical review should challenge.

## 3. The Ten Key Actions: Accountability Mechanism vs. Machinery Without Targets

**The disagreement:** Gemini views the ten actions as providing a strong accountability baseline. Claude and GPT view them as structurally weak because they lack success criteria.

- **Claude** (06-actions.md): "Most are 'launch', 'establish', or 'pilot' actions -- they create structures and programmes rather than delivering tangible results. By March 2027, Scotland will have panels, academies, programmes, and pilots, but the actions do not specify what those programmes will deliver."
- **GPT** (06-actions.md): "Most actions are still framed at a high level. Terms such as 'position', 'launch', 'implement', and 'work with partners' do not yet make clear what will be built, who will own delivery, or how success will be judged."
- **Gemini** (summary.md): "The commitment to delivering ten specific, public-facing actions by March 2027 provides an excellent structural baseline for near-term accountability, contrasting positively with the broader 2031 visions." In the section review: "The commitment to complete these ten highly specific actions 'before the end of March 2027' provides an excellent, rigid timeline for strict governmental accountability."

**Assessment:** This is a significant analytical difference. Claude and GPT distinguish between the existence of a deadline (useful) and the absence of outcome measures (a weakness). Gemini treats the deadline itself as sufficient for "strict governmental accountability." Claude and GPT have the stronger position: a government can tick off all ten actions (launch a programme, establish a panel, publish guidance) without demonstrating any impact. Accountability requires success criteria, not just completion dates. Gemini conflates specificity of timeline with specificity of commitment.

## 4. The Foundations Statistics: Contextualised or Lacking Benchmarks?

**The disagreement:** Claude and GPT both argue that the statistics in the Foundations section lack context and international benchmarks. Gemini treats the same statistics as providing measurable baselines.

- **Claude** (03-foundations.md): "The statistics cited are specific (296 companies, 38.4 TWh, £15 billion investment) but lack context. How does 296 AI companies compare to other small nations? Is 38.4 TWh enough to power the proposed AI infrastructure? The numbers are presented as impressive without benchmarks."
- **GPT** (03-foundations.md): "There are many numbers here, but they are not always contextualised. The counts of companies, renewable output, and headline investment values sound impressive, but the section rarely explains whether these figures are large relative to competitors."
- **Gemini** (03-foundations.md): "It cites specific investment values and gigawatt energy figures, offering baseline metrics that could be evaluated over time."

**Assessment:** Claude and GPT are correct that a number without context is not a benchmark. Knowing Scotland has 296 AI companies tells you nothing about whether this is strong, weak, or average without a comparator. Gemini's observation that the numbers "could be evaluated over time" is true but beside the point -- the question is whether the strategy itself provides sufficient context to judge its starting position, and it does not.

## 5. The GDP Projection: Scrutinised vs. Accepted

**The disagreement:** Claude subjects the headline GDP claim to detailed scrutiny. GPT notes it is conditional but does not challenge it as aggressively. Gemini accepts and amplifies it.

- **Claude** (04-case-for-change.md): "The £23 billion GDP figure is the headline claim of the strategy but warrants scrutiny. The source (endnote 28) is a report commissioned by Scottish Enterprise from GC Insight that is not publicly available. 'With the right investment and leadership' is a significant caveat -- it effectively means the figure is conditional on everything going to plan. The cumulative £140.75 billion figure sounds dramatic but is just the annual figure summed over 10 years, which is a presentational choice designed to impress rather than inform."
- **GPT** (04-case-for-change.md): "The GDP headline is conditional on 'the right investment and leadership', which makes it less a forecast than a scenario."
- **Gemini** (04-case-for-change.md): "Highlights a potential £140.75 billion cumulative GDP boost over a decade if AI is systematically adopted." Gemini does not question the source, methodology, or the cumulative framing. In the summary: "The journey from this practical baseline to the projected £140.75 billion GDP boost is remarkably steep" -- which acknowledges difficulty but still accepts the figure as the target.

**Assessment:** Claude provides the most rigorous treatment, noting the non-public source, the conditional caveat, and the rhetorical function of the cumulative figure. GPT correctly identifies it as a scenario rather than a forecast. Gemini repeats the £140.75 billion cumulative figure without noting that it is simply the annual projection summed ten times -- a presentational inflation that a critical review should flag. Claude's analysis is the strongest on this point.

## 6. AI Scotland's Governance: Clear Leadership vs. Ambiguous Institutional Form

**The disagreement:** Gemini views AI Scotland's governance as clearly defined and practically advantageous. Claude and GPT view the same section as ambiguous and insufficiently specified.

- **Claude** (07-ai-scotland.md): "The relationship between AI Scotland and the ten key actions is ambiguous... Is AI Scotland responsible for delivering all ten actions, or just some? The boundary of its remit is not defined." Claude also flags that deferring the organisational model to year one means the delivery body may not be fully operational until year two.
- **GPT** (07-ai-scotland.md): "It leaves unclear whether it is primarily a programme, a brand, a delivery partnership, or a future standalone institution. That ambiguity matters because each model implies different powers, accountability arrangements, and resourcing needs."
- **Gemini** (07-ai-scotland.md): "The section is highly effective at defining exactly *who* will be leading the execution of the strategy, resolving ambiguities common in high-level policy documents." Gemini also argues that "Utilizing an existing consortium model... significantly increases the feasibility of rapid deployment, as it bypasses the delays of legislating a new public body from scratch."

**Assessment:** This is one of the sharpest disagreements. Claude and GPT identify a genuine structural ambiguity: AI Scotland's institutional form, authority, and accountability are deferred to a future business case. Gemini reads the same text as resolving ambiguity. The text says AI Scotland will determine whether it becomes a cluster management organisation or non-profit company during its first year -- that is definitionally unresolved, not resolved. Claude and GPT have the more defensible reading.

## 7. The Acknowledgments: Narrow Advisory Group vs. Appropriate Mix

**The disagreement:** Claude and GPT flag the advisory group as narrow and unrepresentative. Gemini treats it as appropriate and well-composed.

- **Claude** (19-acknowledgments.md): "Six members is a lean steering group for a national strategy. The group skews towards academia and tech startups -- there is no representation from trade unions, the public sector, healthcare, or the third sector."
- **GPT** (19-acknowledgments.md): "For a national AI strategy, six named advisers is a small core, and the group is weighted toward academia and technology businesses... Trade unions and wider civil society are not named."
- **Gemini** (19-acknowledgments.md): "The explicit naming of the AI Sub-group members... reveals an appropriate mix of private sector enterprise... and academic representation." Gemini specifically praises the inclusion of a "Human Data Interaction" expert as reinforcing the strategy's ethical aims.

**Assessment:** Whether six people constitute an "appropriate mix" depends on the standard applied. For a strategy that repeatedly emphasises Fair Work, public services, trade unions, and inclusion, the absence of any representative from those constituencies in the named advisory group is a legitimate concern. Claude and GPT identify a genuine tension between the strategy's rhetoric and its actual consultation structure. Gemini evaluates the group on its technical expertise alone, which is a narrower lens.

## 8. The Endnotes and Evidence Base: Adequate vs. Domestically Skewed

**The disagreement:** Claude and GPT both flag the evidence base as excessively domestic and lacking international comparative evidence. Gemini finds no major gaps.

- **Claude** (20-endnotes.md): "For a strategy that positions Scotland globally, the references are almost entirely domestic... The only international references are OECD AI principles, the EU AI Act, and a McKinsey report."
- **GPT** (20-endnotes.md): "The source base is heavily domestic. That fits a national strategy, but it means the document cites relatively little comparative international evidence."
- **Gemini** (20-endnotes.md): "The endnotes demonstrate that the document is well-cited and firmly grounded... It also relies on both bespoke commissioned literature and top-tier global research (McKinsey, OECD), supporting a strategic narrative that attempts to balance domestic policy frameworks with global sector insights. No major gaps observed."

**Assessment:** The factual question is answerable: of 55 endnotes, only 3 reference international sources. For a strategy that claims Scotland will be "globally competitive" and a "leading strategic location," this is a thin international evidence base. Gemini's characterisation of the sources as balancing "domestic policy frameworks with global sector insights" overstates the international dimension. Claude and GPT are more accurate.

## 9. The Regulation Section: Market Access Framing vs. Citizen Protection

**The disagreement:** Claude frames the regulation section as primarily about market access rather than citizen protection, identifying this as a gap. GPT and Gemini frame it more favourably as completing the strategy's trust architecture.

- **Claude** (16-layer8-regulation.md): "Regulation is framed as a market access issue rather than a citizen protection issue. It does not reference the responsible AI commitments made in earlier sections (Fair Work, ethical AI, public trust)."
- **GPT** (16-layer8-regulation.md): "This chapter is broadly consistent with the strategy's repeated emphasis on responsible AI and public trust." GPT identifies practical limitations but does not flag the market-access framing as a problem.
- **Gemini** (16-layer8-regulation.md): "This section functionally completes the 'chain of trust' motif introduced in the foreword, providing the formal, structural guardrails conceptually required to deliver the 'Responsible AI' promised throughout the entire strategy."

**Assessment:** This is a genuine interpretive difference. The regulation section's four actions are: advocate for OECD principles, review devolved scope, explore EU AI Act alignment, and publish a report. The EU AI Act discussion focuses explicitly on market access for Scottish businesses selling into Europe. Claude is correct that the section's operative content leans toward market access. GPT and Gemini give it credit for thematic consistency with trust language elsewhere, but that is a different question from whether the section itself builds citizen protection mechanisms. Both readings have merit, but Claude identifies a real analytical gap that the others overlook.

## 10. The Risks Section: Incomplete Register vs. Useful Candour

**The disagreement:** Claude provides a detailed catalogue of missing risks and considers the section seriously incomplete. GPT acknowledges gaps but views the section more charitably. Gemini focuses on the positives of the tabular format.

- **Claude** (17-risks-and-mitigations.md): "Major risks are missing. Misinformation and deepfakes, algorithmic bias beyond gender (race, disability, age, socioeconomic status), cybersecurity threats from AI-enabled attacks, democratic integrity, and concentration of power in a small number of AI companies are all globally recognised AI risks that do not appear here." Claude also flags the absence of execution risk -- what if the strategy itself fails?
- **GPT** (17-risks-and-mitigations.md): "Some important risks receive only limited treatment. The section does not say much about bias beyond gender inequality, nor does it address misinformation, concentration of market power, procurement failure, exclusion of disabled users, or legal redress for harmful decisions. The table is useful, but it feels selective rather than comprehensive."
- **Gemini** (17-risks-and-mitigations.md): "Utilizing a tabular format that maps specific listed risks directly to their corresponding mitigating outcomes provides highly transparent, scannable governance documentation." Gemini does note the omission of racial and socioeconomic bias but does not flag other missing risks.

**Assessment:** All three reviewers agree that gender is too narrow as the only named equality risk. Beyond that, there is a gradient: Claude identifies the most missing risks (misinformation, deepfakes, cybersecurity, democratic integrity, concentration of power, children, execution risk), GPT identifies a subset, and Gemini identifies only algorithmic bias. Claude's analysis is the most thorough, and the missing risks it identifies are well-established in global AI policy discourse (e.g., the EU AI Act itself addresses most of them). This is a case where the most critical review is also the most complete.

## 11. Fair Work: Phantom Commitment vs. Meaningfully Integrated

**The disagreement:** Claude calls Fair Work a "phantom commitment" and identifies it as a strategy-wide failure. GPT treats it as partially integrated. Gemini barely engages with it.

- **Claude** (summary.md): "Fair Work is a phantom commitment. The foreword positions Fair Work as central to Scotland's AI approach. But this commitment is not embedded in delivery structures, governance, or actions. AI Scotland's consortium partners are tech-sector and enterprise-focused with no trade union representation."
- **GPT** (10-layer2-adoption-and-skills.md): "This section is one of the few places where Fair Work is integrated meaningfully rather than mentioned in passing. That makes it more consistent with the foreword than many of the surrounding chapters." GPT acknowledges the gap but credits the Adoption and Skills layer for partially delivering.
- **Gemini**: Across all 21 section reviews and the summary, Gemini does not independently flag the Fair Work follow-through gap. The term appears in Gemini reviews only where it is quoted from the strategy text.

**Assessment:** Claude's analysis is the most thorough and best supported. Tracking a stated commitment across the entire strategy to see whether it is embedded in delivery is exactly the kind of cross-cutting analysis a review should perform. GPT partially does this. Gemini does not perform this analysis at all. The factual question -- is Fair Work embedded in governance structures, actions, and delivery mechanisms? -- can be answered: it is not, beyond one mention in the Adoption and Skills layer and one in the Risks section. Claude's "phantom commitment" characterisation is strong but defensible.

## 12. The Isle of Eigg Case Study: Illustrative vs. Mismatched

**The disagreement:** Claude critiques the Isle of Eigg case study as poorly matched to the Data Centres and Infrastructure section. GPT and Gemini do not flag this mismatch.

- **Claude** (13-layer5-data-centres-and-infrastructure.md): "The case study is the strategy's weakest match to its section. The Isle of Eigg is inspiring but is a 100-person community generating 280 kW of power. A single hyperscale data centre requires 100-500+ MW. The case study demonstrates community-led renewables, not AI infrastructure capability."
- **GPT** (13-layer5-data-centres-and-infrastructure.md): Mentions the case study neutrally as illustrating "community-led renewable infrastructure and local resilience" without flagging the scale mismatch.
- **Gemini** (13-layer5-data-centres-and-infrastructure.md): Does not mention the case study.

**Assessment:** Claude identifies a genuine analytical problem. A case study in an infrastructure section should illustrate the section's argument. The Isle of Eigg generates roughly 1/3,500th of the power a single hyperscale data centre requires. The mismatch between the case study (community-scale renewables) and the section's subject matter (hyperscale AI infrastructure) is a valid criticism that GPT and Gemini miss.

## 13. The Phased Delivery Model: Structurally Sound vs. Effectively a 12-Month Plan

**The disagreement:** Claude argues that because phases 2 and 3 are blank, the strategy is effectively a 12-month action plan branded as a five-year strategy. Gemini views the phased model as building a "predictable rhythm" for accountability. GPT takes a middle position.

- **Claude** (08-ai-action-plan.md): "Phase 2 and phase 3 are blank. The strategy acknowledges this is only phase 1, but provides no indication of what phases 2 and 3 might address. This means the strategy is really a 12-month action plan branded as a five-year strategy."
- **GPT** (08-ai-action-plan.md): "The phased structure is useful, but the section does not specify what each phase is expected to accomplish or how progress from one phase to the next will be assessed."
- **Gemini** (08-ai-action-plan.md): "The phased commitment to publish iterated actions in 2027 and 2029 builds a predictable rhythm for external auditors to assess longitudinal strategy execution."

**Assessment:** Claude's characterisation is factually accurate: only phase 1 actions are defined. Whether this makes the strategy a "12-month plan in a five-year wrapper" or a "phased strategy with built-in review points" is an interpretive choice, but both should acknowledge the same fact. Gemini's framing implies that the phases themselves contain substance, when they are empty commitments to produce future plans. GPT correctly identifies the gap without overstating it.

## 14. Action Repetition: Inflated Volume vs. Strategic Cohesion

**The disagreement:** Claude identifies the repetition of actions across sections as inflating the strategy's apparent volume. Gemini reads the same structural feature as demonstrating strategic cohesion.

- **Claude** (summary.md): "Several actions appear multiple times across sections... This creates an impression of more activity than the strategy actually proposes."
- **Claude** (09-layer1-users.md): "Three of the six actions repeat verbatim from the ten key actions in section 06. This means the Users layer introduces only three new actions beyond what was already committed. The section is thinner than it appears."
- **Gemini** (06-actions.md): "The ten actions map seamlessly back to the four outcome pillars defined in the previous section, demonstrating strong strategic cohesion."

**Assessment:** Both observations can be simultaneously true -- actions can be repeated for cohesion and the repetition can inflate perceived volume. However, Claude's specific analysis is more useful: when the Users layer appears to have six actions but three are repeats from the general actions list, the reader's impression of the section's substance is inflated. This is a legitimate structural criticism. Gemini's "strategic cohesion" framing does not address the fact that repeating an action does not create additional activity.

## 15. The SME Adoption Programme: Concrete KPIs vs. Illustrative Scale Problem

**The disagreement:** Gemini treats the SME programme's figures as "highly concrete, measurable program KPIs." Claude treats the same figures as evidence of how small the programme is relative to the challenge.

- **Gemini** (10-layer2-adoption-and-skills.md): "The commitment of £1 million in funding and the target of over 500 SMEs engaged provide highly concrete, measurable program KPIs."
- **Claude** (10-layer2-adoption-and-skills.md): "The existing programme provides a useful baseline: 500 SMEs engaged out of ~360,000 in Scotland. This is 0.14% of the SME base." Claude notes the absence of targets for the revitalised programme.

**Assessment:** Both are factually correct, but they draw opposite conclusions from the same data. Gemini sees the numbers as evidence of measurability. Claude sees them as evidence of inadequate scale. Claude's analysis is more useful for policy purposes: knowing that the programme reached 0.14% of the SME base contextualises the figure in a way that changes its meaning entirely. A number can be simultaneously concrete and inadequate.

## 16. Devolved Powers and Constitutional Constraints

**The disagreement:** Gemini foregrounds constitutional and external dependencies as a central structural risk throughout its review. Claude and GPT address these constraints section by section but do not elevate them to the same prominence.

- **Gemini** (summary.md): Leads with a warning box: "Several critical structural enablers are situated squarely outside the Scottish Government's direct control." This is presented as one of the strategy's top-level risks.
- **Claude**: Addresses devolved constraints in specific sections (regulation, infrastructure) but frames the strategy's primary weakness as its lack of measurable targets and delivery commitments.
- **GPT**: Similarly addresses devolved constraints in relevant sections but foregrounds measurability and accountability as the dominant weaknesses.

**Assessment:** This is less a disagreement than a difference in emphasis, but it is analytically significant. Gemini's foregrounding of constitutional constraints is a legitimate structural observation -- much of the strategy depends on UK-level cooperation on grid connections, regulation, and industrial policy. Claude and GPT are not wrong to prioritise measurability, but they arguably underweight the external dependency risk. The best analysis would combine both: the strategy lacks measurable targets (Claude/GPT) AND much of what it promises depends on actors outside Scottish Government control (Gemini).

**Assessment:** The omission of "AI" from an AI strategy's glossary is a factual gap, not a matter of interpretive judgement. Claude and GPT correctly identify this. Gemini partially overlaps by noting missing ethical terms but misses the most basic omission.

## 18. The "Unicorn-Scale Valuations" Outcome

**The disagreement:** Claude views the unicorn aspiration as audacious and unsupported by any pathway. Gemini views it as providing an "undeniably unequivocal, binary outcome metric."

- **Claude** (11-layer3-companies-and-products.md): "The aspiration to produce companies 'capable of unicorn-scale valuations' ($1 billion+) is the strategy's most audacious outcome. Scotland has produced very few unicorns in any sector. The section provides no pathway or intermediate milestones."
- **Gemini** (11-layer3-companies-and-products.md): "Aspiring to 'unicorn-scale valuations' provides a very high-bar, but undeniably unequivocal, binary outcome metric."

**Assessment:** Both reviewers note the ambition, but they evaluate it differently. Gemini credits the outcome for being measurable (either you produce a unicorn or you don't). Claude argues that measurability without a delivery pathway is insufficient. Both have a point, but Claude's analysis is more useful: a measurable outcome that lacks any intermediate milestones, supporting actions, or analysis of feasibility is closer to a wish than a target.

## Summary of Patterns

Across these 18 disagreements, a consistent pattern emerges:

1. **Gemini is systematically more favourable** to the strategy than Claude or GPT. In every disagreement, Gemini takes the more positive position. This does not mean Gemini is wrong on every point, but the pattern suggests a less critical analytical lens.

2. **Claude is the most forensic reviewer.** It tracks commitments across sections (Fair Work, action repetition), contextualises statistics (0.14% of SMEs), challenges source material (non-public reports, cumulative GDP framing), and identifies case study mismatches (Isle of Eigg). These are the kinds of cross-cutting observations that distinguish a deep review from a section-by-section summary.

3. **GPT occupies a middle ground** on most disagreements, typically identifying the same issues as Claude but with less sharp characterisation and fewer specific examples.

4. **Claude and GPT agree against Gemini** on the majority of disagreements (Foundations assessment, AI Stack novelty, action measurability, GDP scrutiny, AI Scotland governance, acknowledgments, endnotes, glossary, action repetition, SME programme). This convergence between two independent reviewers strengthens the case for their shared position.

5. **Gemini's unique contribution** is its emphasis on constitutional constraints and external dependencies, which Claude and GPT underweight. This is its strongest analytical addition to the reconciliation.

6. **No reviewer contradicts another on factual matters.** All disagreements are about evaluation, emphasis, and analytical standards. The underlying observations about what the strategy contains are consistent across all three reviews.
