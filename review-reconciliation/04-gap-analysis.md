# Gap Analysis: What None of the Reviewers Covered

This document identifies analytical blind spots -- themes, perspectives, or questions that none of the three reviewers (Claude, GPT, Gemini) substantively addressed. These are not criticisms of the reviews, but an acknowledgment that three LLMs using the same methodology and checklist will tend to share the same field of vision.

## 1. Comparative international benchmarking

All three reviewers noted that the *strategy itself* lacks international comparisons. But none of the reviewers actually performed that comparison either. No reviewer benchmarked Scotland's strategy against peer national AI strategies (e.g. Finland's AuroraAI, Denmark's National Strategy for AI, Ireland's AI strategy, Estonia's AI action plan, or Wales/Northern Ireland equivalents). Doing so would have revealed whether Scotland's gaps are common to small-nation strategies or unique weaknesses, and whether the proposed actions are ambitious or conservative by international standards.

## 2. Inter-strategy coherence

The strategy references at least 10 other Scottish Government strategies and frameworks (Digital Strategy, NSET, Public Service Reform, Life Sciences 2035, Green Industrial Strategy, Fair Work, Cyber Resilience Framework, etc.). No reviewer systematically checked whether the AI strategy's commitments are consistent with, or already covered by, these adjacent strategies. This matters because:
- Some "new" actions may already be in train under other strategies
- Conflicting priorities across strategies could constrain delivery
- The AI strategy may be relying on assumptions from other strategies that are themselves under-delivered

## 3. Political and institutional dynamics

The reviews treated the strategy as a technical policy document. None examined:
- **Political incentives**: Why the strategy is structured to maximise announceability (10 actions, named programmes) while minimising binding commitments
- **Election cycle**: Whether the 2027 phase 1 deadline aligns with political cycles and what happens to continuity under a different government
- **Institutional politics**: The relationships and potential tensions between Scottish Enterprise, The Data Lab, ScotlandIS, SDS, SFC, and universities -- all of whom must cooperate for delivery
- **UK-Scotland political dynamics**: Beyond the regulatory section, how the broader UK-Scotland relationship affects AI policy delivery (e.g. shared competency areas, Barnett funding implications)

## 4. Stakeholder power analysis

No reviewer examined who benefits and who loses from the strategy's choices:
- The strategy heavily favours the tech sector and university research base. What about sectors that will be disrupted by AI (retail, call centres, administrative roles)?
- SMEs are the target of adoption programmes, but large enterprises employ far more people and face different challenges -- this was noted by Claude but not analysed in depth by any reviewer
- Rural communities are mentioned but no reviewer assessed whether the strategy's actions are genuinely accessible outside the Central Belt
- Third sector organisations are largely absent from the strategy and from the reviews

## 5. Fiscal context

All three reviewers flagged the absence of budgets in the strategy. But none placed this in the context of Scotland's current fiscal position -- public spending constraints, the Scottish Budget process, or what competing demands exist for the same funding. The £1 million SME programme and £859,648 CARES award are the only figures in the strategy; no reviewer assessed whether the scale of ambition is achievable within realistic public expenditure scenarios.

## 6. Implementation evidence

No reviewer drew on evidence from implementation science or previous Scottish Government strategy delivery:
- What is Scotland's track record on delivering previous strategies of this type?
- The 2021 AI Strategy is referenced -- what was actually delivered from that strategy, and what wasn't?
- What does the evidence say about the success rate of government "pilots", "panels", and "advisory boards" as delivery mechanisms?

## 7. Citizen and public perspective

The strategy claims to prioritise public trust and citizen engagement. All three reviewers noted this is under-developed. But none examined:
- What existing public opinion data says about Scottish attitudes to AI
- Whether the proposed "nationwide engagement programme" has precedent and what similar programmes achieved
- The perspective of people who will interact with AI-enabled public services involuntarily (benefits claimants, patients, social care recipients)

## 8. Shared methodology bias

All three reviewers used the same checklist (clarity, measurability, gaps, feasibility, internal consistency) and the same section-by-section structure. This creates systematic blind spots:
- **Cross-cutting themes** that don't sit neatly in one section may be under-examined (e.g. Gaelic language considerations, rural digital divide, islands communities impact)
- **Cumulative effects** across sections are harder to spot in section-by-section review
- **What the strategy gets right** may be under-reported because the checklist is oriented toward identifying weaknesses
- All three LLMs are trained on similar data and may share similar analytical frameworks, potentially missing perspectives that would come from domain experts, affected communities, or practitioners

## 9. Legal and human rights framework

The strategy references the OECD principles and EU AI Act, but no reviewer assessed:
- Compatibility with the Scotland-specific legal framework (e.g. Scots law, the Human Rights Act as applied in Scotland, the UNCRC incorporation)
- Whether the proposed AI framework for health and social care is consistent with existing NHS governance and clinical safety requirements
- Data protection implications under UK GDPR for the proposed data matchmaking pilot

## 10. Environmental impact assessment

All reviewers noted the tension between data centre energy demands and environmental commitments. But none examined:
- Whether the strategy's environmental claims about renewables are consistent with current grid capacity and curtailment data
- The water consumption implications in specific proposed locations
- Whether an SEA (Strategic Environmental Assessment) is required for the strategy and whether one has been conducted

## Summary

The three reviews converge on a strong set of findings about the strategy's internal qualities (clarity, measurability, governance). The shared gaps above are mostly about *external context* -- the political, fiscal, legal, and comparative landscape that determines whether the strategy can actually deliver. This is a natural limitation of reviewing a document in isolation, and suggests that a subsequent review phase could usefully bring in domain expertise and real-world context that LLM-based text analysis alone cannot provide.
