# Consensus Findings: Three-LLM Review of Scotland's AI Strategy 2026-2031

## What this document is

Three independent LLM reviewers -- Claude, GPT, and Gemini -- each conducted a section-by-section review of Scotland's AI Strategy 2026-2031 without knowledge of each other's work. This document identifies **consensus findings**: points where all three reviewers independently arrived at the same conclusion. These are the highest-confidence observations, since each emerged from a separate analytical process applied to the same source material.

Where a finding was raised by all three reviewers but framed differently, the framing of each is noted. Where two of three agreed but the third was silent or divergent, the finding is excluded.

---

## 1. Measurability: The Strategy's Most Persistent Weakness

**All three reviewers independently identified the absence of measurable targets, baselines, and success criteria as the strategy's central and most damaging flaw.** This was raised not just at the summary level but repeatedly in individual section reviews across the entire document.

- **Claude** called it "the strategy's most fundamental weakness," noting that "none of the eight high-level outcomes, and none of the layer-specific outcomes, include a target, threshold, metric, or baseline." Phrases like "widespread literacy," "globally competitive," and "world-leading research" were cited as inherently unassessable. Claude concluded that "the government could claim success on every outcome in 2031 without demonstrating meaningful change."

- **GPT** described measurability as "the most persistent weakness across the strategy," noting that "the document contains some strong headline numbers, but they are often not contextualised well enough to judge significance" and that "the strategy uses precise language when describing some current conditions, but becomes imprecise when defining future success."

- **Gemini** observed that "the ultimate 2031 outcomes (such as 'widespread literacy', 'equality of access', or 'strong technology clusters') remain rhetorical and qualitative" and recommended "translating these into strict, quantitative KPIs to track longitudinal progress objectively."

All three reached this conclusion independently in their reviews of the Purpose and Outcomes section (05), the Actions section (06), and in virtually every AI Stack layer review. The consensus is unambiguous: the strategy lacks the specificity needed to hold itself accountable.

---

## 2. Actions Create Structures, Not Results

**All three reviewers observed that the strategy's actions predominantly establish new bodies, panels, pilots, and programmes, rather than committing to tangible deliverables.**

- **Claude** noted that "the majority of the strategy's actions establish structures: panels, boards, academies, pilots, reviews, and reports. Very few commit to delivering a specific, tangible outcome. By March 2027, Scotland will have launched programmes and constituted advisory bodies, but the strategy does not specify what those programmes should achieve."

- **GPT** observed that "several actions commit to launching initiatives or producing reports, but fewer commit to clearly measurable outputs or outcomes." On the Actions section specifically: "Terms such as 'position', 'launch', 'implement', and 'work with partners' do not yet make clear what will be built, who will own delivery, or how success will be judged."

- **Gemini** raised the same point indirectly, noting that "delivering ten substantial national initiatives in essentially a single year indicates an extremely aggressive public sector timeline" while also noting the distinction between launching something and achieving something.

The consensus is that the strategy's action plan is primarily a list of things to set up, not a list of things to achieve.

---

## 3. No Budgets or Resource Commitments

**All three reviewers noted the complete absence of funding figures, cost estimates, or resource allocations attached to the strategy's actions.**

- **Claude** stated: "Not a single action in the strategy includes a cost, funding source, or resource allocation." The only figure cited across the entire strategy is "nearly £1 million" for the existing SME AI Adoption Programme.

- **GPT** noted that "the strategy often assumes that coordination, convening, or partnership-building will be enough to overcome structural barriers without showing what additional powers, funding, or institutional change will be required." In the Actions section review: "there is little on funding, sequencing, or delivery dependencies."

- **Gemini** observed the same gap in the Purpose and Outcomes section: "The document articulates *what* the outcomes are but omits any mention of the funding or resource strategy that will be required to bridge the gap between present day and the 2031 vision."

All three reviewers connected this absence to the credibility of the strategy's ambitions. A strategy that proposes an AI Leadership Academy, a Scale-up Accelerator, a nationwide engagement programme, and a data matchmaking pilot without indicating how any of them will be funded is difficult to take at face value.

---

## 4. The Case for Change Is the Strongest Section

**All three reviewers independently singled out the Case for Change as the most honest, grounded, and analytically strong part of the strategy.**

- **Claude** called it "the strongest section so far in terms of honesty," noting that "it acknowledges real problems: low adoption, fragmented data, skills shortages, and infrastructure pressure. The tone shifts from the promotional style of the Foundations section to something more analytical."

- **GPT** described it as "one of the strongest sections" and "one of the more grounded sections in the document" because "it identifies several concrete pressures at once: adoption gaps, data fragmentation, infrastructure demands, skills shortages, and geopolitical competition."

- **Gemini** called the section "highly lucid, effectively setting out a 'carrot and stick' argument that balances the incentive of GDP growth with the risk of falling behind globally."

The consensus is that the strategy is at its best when diagnosing problems and at its weakest when prescribing solutions. The candour of the Case for Change creates expectations that subsequent sections struggle to meet.

---

## 5. The Foundations Section Reads as a Promotional Prospectus, Not a Balanced Assessment

**All three reviewers found that the Foundations section functions more as a marketing document for Scotland's strengths than as a genuine assessment of the country's starting position.**

- **Claude** stated it "reads more like an investment prospectus than a strategy foundation" and that "a 'foundations' section that only lists assets gives an incomplete picture of the starting position." Specific concerns included the absence of weaknesses, the lack of benchmarking for statistics, and the omission of skills and education.

- **GPT** agreed that the section "functions more as a promotional inventory than an assessment of foundations. It names assets and success stories, yet gives very little sense of structural weakness, dependency, or constraint." GPT noted that "the main omission is a serious account of what Scotland lacks."

- **Gemini** was more positive about the section's clarity and specificity but still noted it "does not immediately detail the structural infrastructure challenges" and that key constraints were deferred to later sections.

The consensus across Claude and GPT is explicit; Gemini is softer but aligned in identifying the absence of weakness analysis as a gap.

---

## 6. The Semiconductor Section Is the Strongest Strategic Thinking

**All three reviewers praised the Semiconductors section for its realistic, niche-focused strategy -- avoiding the trap of overreach.**

- **Claude** called it "one of the clearest sections in the strategy" and said "the niche positioning is explicit and realistic -- Scotland is not trying to be Taiwan or South Korea. The focus on specialised capabilities (photonics, advanced packaging, superconducting components) rather than mass fabrication is strategically sound and well articulated."

- **GPT** agreed: "The section is stronger than it might have been because it does not claim Scotland will become a full-spectrum semiconductor power. Instead, it makes a more focused case for niche leadership in selected segments where Scotland already has capability."

- **Gemini** observed that the strategy "very astutely narrows the strategic focus to 'niche strengths' (photonics, advanced packaging), avoiding the trap of promising impossible competition directly against global semiconductor fabrication behemoths."

All three also noted that even this strong section suffers from the strategy-wide weakness of vague outcomes without measurable targets.

---

## 7. Fair Work Is Rhetorically Central but Operationally Absent

**All three reviewers tracked the "Fair Work" commitment from the foreword through the entire strategy and found it was not embedded in governance, delivery, or accountability structures.**

- **Claude** identified Fair Work as "a phantom commitment," noting that "the foreword positions Fair Work as central to Scotland's AI approach. But this commitment is not embedded in delivery structures, governance, or actions. AI Scotland's consortium partners are tech-sector and enterprise-focused with no trade union representation."

- **GPT** observed that "Fair Work is important in the framing, but outside the skills and workforce material it is often present more as language than as an operational standard." In the Adoption and Skills review, GPT noted it was "one of the few places where Fair Work is integrated meaningfully rather than mentioned in passing."

- **Gemini** noted that the foreword "does not elaborate on what [Fair Work] actually entails in the context of an AI-driven economic transition."

All three reviewers also noted that the glossary omits a definition of Fair Work despite its prominence in the strategy. The consensus is that Fair Work is used as a framing device rather than an actionable policy commitment.

---

## 8. The Research-to-Commercialisation Gap Is Correctly Diagnosed but Inadequately Addressed

**All three reviewers agreed that the strategy honestly identifies Scotland's failure to convert research excellence into commercial outcomes, but that its proposed interventions may be insufficient.**

- **Claude** praised the section for its honesty ("the most honest opening in the strategy") but noted "no diagnosis of why commercialisation fails" and that "the proposed interventions (cluster scheme, Venture Creator) may not address the actual barriers" without that diagnosis.

- **GPT** agreed: "The diagnosis is clear and plausible: Scotland is strong at producing research and weaker at extracting wider commercial benefit from it" but the section "says less than it should about incentives inside universities, procurement barriers in the public sector, and the balance between retaining intellectual property domestically and seeking global scale."

- **Gemini** also noted the frank admission was "a refreshingly realistic starting point" but warned that "radically altering university commercialisation processes often requires navigating deep-seated institutional inertia, intense academic IP complexities, and risk-averse university administrations."

The consensus is threefold: the diagnosis is correct, it is welcomed as honest, and the proposed remedies are not commensurate with the depth of the problem.

---

## 9. Public Data Fragmentation Is a Systemic Problem Met with Incremental Remedies

**All three reviewers agreed that public sector data fragmentation is correctly identified as a critical barrier, but that the proposed actions are small relative to the scale of the problem.**

- **Claude** noted: "The problem statement is clear and honest: data is fragmented, access is slow, and Scotland risks falling behind. However, the solutions are generic." The action to "work with public sector organisations to identify the barriers" was flagged as an action to understand the problem rather than solve it.

- **GPT** observed that "the distance between pilots and a mature, coordinated public-sector data ecosystem is large. Achieving that will likely require more than coordination; it will need persistent standards work, shared infrastructure, and strong governance authority."

- **Gemini** warned that "historically, overcoming deeply entrenched data silos across multifarious independent local governments and NHS health boards is notoriously difficult, both technically and culturally. Delivering this will require immense political capital."

All three agreed the problem is real and important; all three questioned whether coordination and pilots can overcome decades of institutional fragmentation.

---

## 10. Grid Infrastructure Is a Critical Dependency Outside Scottish Control

**All three reviewers flagged that the strategy's infrastructure ambitions depend heavily on UK-reserved powers, particularly grid connections.**

- **Claude** noted that "grid connection timescales are acknowledged as a bottleneck; this is a UK-reserved policy area" and that "the grid connection bottleneck is the critical constraint, and the section honestly acknowledges it while also admitting it is outside Scotland's direct control."

- **GPT** observed that "the document is reasonably realistic in noting that key levers are reserved to the UK, but that also exposes how constrained Scottish control is over the agenda."

- **Gemini** identified this as "a pivotal external dependency that resides outside the direct legislative power of the Scottish Government," part of a broader pattern where "several critical structural enablers are situated squarely outside the Scottish Government's direct control."

The consensus is that the strategy deserves credit for acknowledging this constraint, but the acknowledgment also reveals how limited Scotland's agency is over one of the strategy's most important enabling conditions.

---

## 11. Regulation: Clear Direction, Limited Practical Agency

**All three reviewers agreed that the regulation section has a clear policy position (OECD-aligned, EU-compatible) but that Scotland's ability to deliver on this position is constitutionally constrained.**

- **Claude** called it "the most politically significant section" and noted that all four actions "are studies, reviews, or advocacy positions. None creates a regulatory change."

- **GPT** observed: "The section is realistic to the extent that it acknowledges shared competence and the importance of UK-wide coordination. But that realism also limits the power of its promises."

- **Gemini** noted the "heavy reliance on 'advocating' for a specific UK Government statutory approach highlights the reality that Scotland's regulatory autonomy regarding sweeping tech regulation is constitutionally constrained."

All three also agreed that the pro-EU alignment position is clearly articulated and strategically coherent. The consensus is that the regulatory section is analytically strong but operationally limited by devolved powers.

---

## 12. The Risks Section Is Incomplete

**All three reviewers found the risk register to be selective and high-level, with important omissions.**

- **Claude** identified specific missing risks: "misinformation and deepfakes, algorithmic bias beyond gender (race, disability, age), cybersecurity threats from AI-enabled attacks, democratic integrity, concentration of power in a small number of AI companies, and risks to children and young people."

- **GPT** agreed that "the section does not say much about bias beyond gender inequality, nor does it address misinformation, concentration of market power, procurement failure, exclusion of disabled users, or legal redress for harmful decisions."

- **Gemini** noted specifically that the section "surprisingly omits broader mention of racial or socioeconomic algorithmic biases, which are equally vital components of the global AI ethics discourse."

All three also agreed that the mitigations are framed as desired outcomes rather than actionable interventions. The consensus is that gender inequality as the sole named equality risk is a notable gap given the breadth of the Equality Act and global AI ethics discourse.

---

## 13. The AI Stack Is a Useful Framework

**All three reviewers found the AI Stack to be a reasonable and effective organising model for the strategy, while noting it is not as novel as the strategy claims.**

- **Claude** acknowledged it as "a useful organising structure" but pushed back on calling it "radical," noting that "layered models for technology ecosystems are well-established."

- **GPT** agreed: "The AI Stack provides a workable organising frame for the rest of the document, even if it is not fully justified as a novel policy model."

- **Gemini** was the most positive, calling it "a highly resonant and logically robust framework" and praising the placement of Data and Regulation as encircling layers as "conceptually stellar."

The consensus is that the AI Stack works well as a structuring device. Claude and GPT are sceptical of the "radical" framing; Gemini accepts it more readily.

---

## 14. Case Studies Are a Clear Strength

**All three reviewers consistently praised the strategy's use of case studies as the most concrete and persuasive element of the document.**

- **Claude** highlighted Citizens Advice Scotland (35,000 cases), EGG Lighting (£40-60k cost avoidance), and Chemify ($50 million raise, 180 jobs) as "strong, specific examples of responsible AI adoption" that "demonstrate what the strategy could look like if it were more concrete throughout."

- **GPT** noted the case studies bring real commercial traction and measurable results to the narrative, particularly in the Adoption and Skills and Innovation layers.

- **Gemini** praised specific examples like the Chemify case study and the use of concrete investment and job-creation figures throughout.

The consensus creates an ironic finding: the strategy's case studies demonstrate the kind of measurability and specificity that the strategy itself fails to apply to its own actions and outcomes.

---

## 15. The SME Adoption Programme Is Promising but Undersized

**All three reviewers agreed that the existing SME AI Adoption Programme provides a credible foundation but is too small relative to the challenge.**

- **Claude** noted that the programme reached "500+ SMEs" out of approximately 360,000 in Scotland -- "0.14% of the SME base" -- and that "the section does not state what the revitalised programme aims to achieve."

- **GPT** agreed: "The harder question is whether these interventions are large enough to shift adoption behaviour across the whole Scottish economy rather than helping a relatively small number of firms at the margin."

- **Gemini** observed that "while the £1 million investment is a positive catalyst, spreading this sum across a national SME business base is relatively modest, which may limit the depth of sustained 'hands-on assistance' available to individual firms."

The consensus is that the programme is a good model that needs dramatically more resources to match the strategy's ambitions.

---

## 16. Consultation Was Narrow Relative to the Strategy's Scope

**All three reviewers noted that the named contributors and advisory group are weighted toward technology and academia, underrepresenting stakeholders central to the strategy's themes.**

- **Claude** observed that the six-member AI Sub-group "skews towards academia and tech startups -- there is no representation from trade unions, the public sector, healthcare, or the third sector."

- **GPT** agreed: "The named leadership group is narrow. For a national AI strategy, six named advisers is a small core, and the group is weighted toward academia and technology businesses. Trade unions and wider civil society are not named."

- **Gemini** did not raise this as a concern in the acknowledgments review, but this is a clear consensus between Claude and GPT, with Gemini silent rather than disagreeing.

---

## 17. The Glossary Omits the Strategy's Own Central Terms

**All three reviewers flagged that the glossary fails to define terms that are central to the strategy itself.**

- **Claude** noted that "AI" itself is not defined, nor are "AI Stack," "Fair Work," or "Artificial General Intelligence."

- **GPT** agreed that "AI, AI Stack, and Fair Work are all central to the document but are not defined."

- **Gemini** observed that "terminology frequently leaned on throughout the strategy, such as 'Trustworthy AI', 'Responsible AI', or even specific Scottish frameworks like 'Fair Work', are surprisingly absent from the glossary."

The consensus is that the glossary supports the technical material but leaves the strategy's own core concepts undefined.
