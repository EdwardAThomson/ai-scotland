# Review: Layer 6 - Semiconductors

## Summary

This section argues that semiconductors, photonics, and related hardware capabilities are strategically important to Scotland's AI future and offer a route to focused competitive advantage. Rather than claiming Scotland can compete across the entire semiconductor chain, it points to niche strengths in critical technologies, research, packaging, and low-energy hardware.

## Key Points

- The strategy presents semiconductors and related microprocessor technologies as essential to the AI era.
- Scotland's opportunity is framed around niche strengths in photonics, power electronics, advanced packaging, connectivity, sensing, and quantum-adjacent technologies.
- The section highlights the Critical Technologies supercluster, more than 50 companies in the semiconductor cluster, and over `£1.2 billion` in annual turnover.
- It argues that other countries are already integrating semiconductor capability into national AI strategies, creating competitive pressure.
- The 2027 actions focus on coordinating strengths through the CTSAB, enabling spin-outs, and aligning workforce planning to industry needs.
- The 2031 outcomes focus on cluster growth, spin-out support, inward investment, and research leadership.
- The Quantcore case study is used to illustrate local fabrication capability and reduced dependency on overseas supply chains.

## Observations

- **Clarity** -- The section is stronger than it might have been because it does not claim Scotland will become a full-spectrum semiconductor power. Instead, it makes a more focused case for niche leadership in selected segments where Scotland already has capability.
- **Measurability** -- Despite that relative focus, the outcomes are still not well defined. Terms such as `global success`, `leading strategic location`, and `sovereign capability` are too elastic to function as meaningful performance tests without concrete indicators for investment, output, jobs, or production capacity.
- **Gaps** -- The section underexplains the scale of capital intensity and geopolitical dependency in semiconductors. Even if Scotland has real strengths in design, photonics, or advanced packaging, the broader supply chain remains highly international and vulnerable to forces far outside devolved control. That reality deserves more attention.
- **Feasibility** -- The proposed actions are modest compared with the scale of the sector, which may be appropriate. Coordination, spin-out support, and workforce alignment are all sensible, but they are unlikely on their own to transform Scotland's position unless matched by sustained long-term investment and deep collaboration with UK and international partners.
- **Internal consistency** -- This section fits reasonably well with the strategy's broader preference for selective, strengths-based positioning rather than trying to compete everywhere. It also supports the infrastructure and innovation layers. But, as elsewhere, the causal jump from coordinated ecosystem support to durable strategic advantage is asserted more than demonstrated.
