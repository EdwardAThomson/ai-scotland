# GPT Findings Summary

This document summarises the main findings from the GPT review of Scotland's AI Strategy 2026-2031.

## Overall finding

The strategy is strongest when it names real structural challenges, especially around low adoption, weak research commercialisation, fragmented public-sector data, infrastructure constraints, and the need for public trust. It is weaker when it tries to convert those challenges into measurable outcomes, hard delivery choices, or clear accountability mechanisms.

## Main findings

- The strategy is consistently ambitious, but much of that ambition is expressed in high-level language rather than in specific, testable commitments.
- Many outcomes are framed as desirable end states such as `inclusive`, `trusted`, `globally competitive`, or `world-leading`, but they are rarely tied to baselines, thresholds, or indicators.
- The document is better at describing direction of travel than at defining what success or failure would look like by 2027 or 2031.
- A recurring pattern is that principles are stated clearly, while delivery mechanisms remain comparatively thin.

## Strengths identified

- The strategy has a clear narrative about why AI matters to Scotland and why action is needed now.
- The `Case for Change` section is one of the strongest parts of the document because it identifies concrete pressures rather than relying only on promotional language.
- The strategy has a coherent interest in linking AI growth to public value, responsible use, and trust rather than treating growth alone as the goal.
- Some sections, especially `Adoption and Skills`, are more grounded and practical because they connect technology adoption to leadership, workforce capability, and Fair Work.
- The AI Stack provides a workable organising frame for the rest of the document, even if it is not fully justified as a novel policy model.
- The document recognises several real enabling constraints, including data access, infrastructure, skills, and regulation.

## Main weaknesses identified

- The outcomes are often too vague to function as meaningful tests of progress.
- The strategy rarely specifies who owns delivery, how decisions will be made, or how accountability will operate across the many bodies involved.
- Public trust is treated as central, but the document provides limited detail on redress, challenge mechanisms, enforcement, or what happens when AI use causes harm.
- Fair Work is important in the framing, but outside the skills and workforce material it is often present more as language than as an operational standard.
- The strategy often assumes that coordination, convening, or partnership-building will be enough to overcome structural barriers without showing what additional powers, funding, or institutional change will be required.
- A number of sections rely on reputational or comparative claims such as `leader`, `credible player`, or `competitive strength` without explaining the benchmark.

## Findings by theme

### Governance and accountability

- The strategy creates or references multiple programmes, boards, panels, pilots, and partnerships, but governance arrangements are often not fully defined.
- `AI Scotland` is central to delivery, yet its long-term form, authority, and accountability are left unsettled.
- Several actions commit to launching initiatives or producing reports, but fewer commit to clearly measurable outputs or outcomes.

### Measurability and evidence

- This is the most persistent weakness across the strategy.
- The document contains some strong headline numbers, but they are often not contextualised well enough to judge significance.
- The strategy uses precise language when describing some current conditions, but becomes imprecise when defining future success.
- Progress reporting is promised, but the document gives little detail on what metrics will actually be used.

### Economic growth and competitiveness

- The strategy strongly favours a growth narrative based on research strength, AI company formation, infrastructure, and inward investment.
- In several places it overstates how far ecosystem coordination alone can close the gap between Scotland and larger AI centres.
- The ambition to be a producer of AI, not only a consumer, is coherent, but the strategy is less convincing about how much of that agenda is practically within Scottish control.

### Research, innovation, and commercialisation

- The strategy correctly identifies a gap between research excellence and commercial impact.
- It is stronger on diagnosis than on explaining why earlier commercialisation efforts have not gone far enough.
- Public-sector demand is presented as a possible driver of innovation, but the document is still thin on procurement reform and market-shaping detail.

### Data, trust, and public services

- The strategy recognises that trustworthy AI in public services depends on better data-sharing, stronger governance, and public understanding.
- It is strong on the importance of trust, transparency, and literacy, but weaker on the institutional means of securing those outcomes.
- The data chapter identifies the right constraints, especially fragmentation and slow access, but the proposed remedies still look relatively incremental compared with the scale of the problem.

### Infrastructure and sustainability

- The infrastructure sections are among the more realistic parts of the strategy because they acknowledge water, grid, and planning constraints.
- There is a visible tension between attracting large-scale AI infrastructure and ensuring environmental responsibility, local benefit, and community consent.
- The strategy recognises this tension but does not fully resolve it.

### Regulation

- The regulatory section gives a reasonably clear statement of intent around OECD principles, EU alignment, and trust.
- Its main limitation is practical agency: much of what matters sits at UK or international level, and the document is not always explicit about what Scotland can directly deliver.
- Regulatory ambition is therefore more persuasive as a position than as an implementation plan.

### Risks

- The risks section is usefully candid that not all risks are yet known.
- It identifies important themes, but the list of risks and mitigations still feels selective and high level.
- Across the strategy as a whole, risks are often named more clearly than they are operationally managed.

## Section-level findings

- `Foreword` and `Introduction`: clear framing, but highly aspirational and dependent on later sections to supply operational meaning.
- `Foundations`: strong catalogue of assets, but too one-sided to function as a balanced baseline.
- `Case for Change`: one of the strongest sections because it names real problems and pressures.
- `Purpose and Outcomes`: central weakness of the strategy because the outcomes are too broad to support robust evaluation.
- `Actions`: useful step toward operationalisation, but still underspecified on ownership, funding, and success measures.
- `AI Scotland`: central delivery vehicle, but governance and institutional form remain unclear.
- `AI Action Plan`: coherent organising frame, but still weak on prioritisation and phase-level criteria.
- `Users`: persuasive on legitimacy and trust, but underdeveloped on challenge and redress.
- `Adoption and Skills`: comparatively strong and more practical than many other sections.
- `Companies and Products`: clear ambition, but limited realism about structural constraints and scale.
- `Innovation and R&D`: solid diagnosis of the commercialisation gap, but delivery tools may be too soft.
- `Data Centres and Infrastructure`: realistic about physical constraints, but vague on trade-offs and sovereignty.
- `Semiconductors`: focused and more credible because it leans into niche strengths, though still highly aspirational.
- `Data`: good diagnosis, but incremental remedies for a systemic problem.
- `Regulation`: clear in direction, limited in direct leverage.
- `Risks and Mitigations`: useful acknowledgement of uncertainty, but mitigations remain broad.

## Findings on reference sections

- The contents and reference material are broadly serviceable for review purposes.
- The glossary is useful but incomplete, especially for some of the strategy's own central terms.
- The acknowledgments suggest breadth of engagement, but the named contributors do not fully reflect the full range of stakeholders implied by the strategy.
- The endnotes provide some supporting evidence, but several citations are thin and the source base is still heavily domestic.
