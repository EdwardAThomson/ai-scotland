# Review: Foundations

## Summary

This section is the strategy's evidence-building and confidence-building chapter. It assembles a broad case that Scotland already has meaningful assets in AI research, energy, business formation, public-sector use, and sectoral specialism, and that these assets provide a strong base for the rest of the strategy.

## Key Points

- Scotland is presented as having a strong historical and academic lineage in AI through named pioneers and major universities.
- The strategy claims Scotland has world-leading AI research capacity, with five universities in the UK's top 30 for AI research output in 2025.
- Scotland's renewable electricity base is presented as a strategic advantage for AI development.
- The section cites an estimated 296 AI-focused companies and highlights several fast-growing firms and major investments.
- Public-sector AI adoption is framed as important both for service improvement and for building public trust.
- Six sectoral areas are identified where Scotland is said to have competitive strength: healthcare and life sciences, advanced manufacturing and robotics, financial services and fintech, renewable energy and climate science, creative industries, and space and satellite technology.
- Enterprise agencies, inward investment activity, and the Scottish National Investment Bank are presented as key enablers.

## Observations

- **Clarity** -- The section is clear about what Scotland thinks its strengths are, but it functions more as a promotional inventory than an assessment of foundations. It names assets and success stories, yet gives very little sense of structural weakness, dependency, or constraint.
- **Measurability** -- There are many numbers here, but they are not always contextualised. The counts of companies, renewable output, and headline investment values sound impressive, but the section rarely explains whether these figures are large relative to competitors, sufficient for the strategy's ambitions, or secure rather than provisional.
- **Gaps** -- The main omission is a serious account of what Scotland lacks. There is little on constraints around access to capital, compute, skills bottlenecks, procurement barriers, or regional inequality in AI capability. For a section called `Foundations`, that absence matters because a strategy should be built on a realistic starting diagnosis, not just a list of strengths.
- **Feasibility** -- The section implies that research strength, renewable energy, and selective investment can combine into global competitiveness, but it does not explain the mechanism by which these assets will be converted into sustained advantage. That is especially important given the scale gap between Scotland and larger AI centres.
- **Internal consistency** -- The wider strategy repeatedly stresses responsible AI, Fair Work, and trust, but those themes are not deeply embedded in this section. Public-service examples and one or two research references touch on social value, yet the overall framing remains closer to an economic pitch than to a balanced foundation for a responsible AI strategy.
