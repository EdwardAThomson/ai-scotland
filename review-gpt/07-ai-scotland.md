# Review: AI Scotland

## Summary

This section establishes `AI Scotland` as the main delivery mechanism for the strategy and places it at the centre of coordination across business, academia, and the public sector. It also signals that the programme's long-term organisational form is not yet settled and will be worked up through a future business case.

## Key Points

- `AI Scotland` is presented as the national transformation programme that will drive strategy delivery.
- The programme will be led by the Scottish Government together with partners including The Data Lab, ScotlandIS, and enterprise agencies.
- An expanded SME-focused AI Adoption Programme will be an early intervention under the AI Scotland banner.
- All AI Scotland programmes are said to reflect commitments to responsible and ethical AI.
- An Expert Advisory Board will be established to provide accountability and shape priorities.
- The Advisory Board will also inform a first-year business case on the preferred long-term organisational model for AI Scotland.
- Potential future models include a cluster management organisation or a non-profit company.

## Observations

- **Clarity** -- The section makes clear that AI Scotland is meant to be the central coordinating vehicle, but it leaves unclear whether it is primarily a programme, a brand, a delivery partnership, or a future standalone institution. That ambiguity matters because each model implies different powers, accountability arrangements, and resourcing needs.
- **Measurability** -- The section contains very few concrete commitments beyond establishing the programme and advisory structures. There are no clear success measures for AI Scotland itself, no milestones for the adoption programme, and no criteria for judging whether the chosen organisational model is working.
- **Gaps** -- Governance is the main gap. The section says AI Scotland will ensure accountability, but it does not explain who the programme is accountable to, how decisions will be made, how conflicts between partners will be handled, or how public-interest concerns will be represented alongside industry priorities.
- **Feasibility** -- Building a major national delivery vehicle while also deciding its eventual institutional form later is workable, but it carries risk. A programme that begins operating before its long-term governance and mandate are settled may struggle with focus, authority, or continuity.
- **Internal consistency** -- The emphasis on responsible and ethical AI aligns with the strategy's stated values, but the `triple helix` framing again privileges business, academia, and the public sector. That sits somewhat uneasily with earlier claims about inclusion, public trust, and Fair Work, which imply a broader set of stakeholders than this section makes visible.
