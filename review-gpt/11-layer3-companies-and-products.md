# Review: Layer 3 - Companies and Products

## Summary

This section makes the case that Scotland should aim to be a producer of AI, not merely a user of it. It links the growth of domestic AI companies to sovereignty, productivity, investment, talent retention, and long-term economic resilience, and proposes a set of interventions aimed at helping firms scale.

## Key Points

- Scotland is said to have the entrepreneurial, research, and support base needed to produce world-leading AI companies.
- The strategy connects domestic AI company growth to control over data, models, infrastructure, and economic value.
- It argues that successful AI firms create high-productivity jobs and help retain talent in Scotland.
- The 2027 actions include a scale-up accelerator, a national ecosystem event, a company-needs assessment, work on access to compute, and stronger investor engagement.
- The 2031 outcomes focus on a globally competitive AI sector, investment attraction, responsible innovation, and access to compute and infrastructure.
- The Gigged.AI case study is used to show commercial traction, measurable enterprise benefits, and attention to transparency, data quality, and compliance.

## Observations

- **Clarity** -- The section is clear about its ambition: Scotland wants more AI firms that scale successfully and remain rooted domestically. It also usefully distinguishes between being a producer and being only a consumer of AI technologies.
- **Measurability** -- The outcomes remain highly aspirational. Terms such as `credible player`, `world-class products`, and `unicorn-scale valuations` have rhetorical force, but they are not adequate performance measures. The strategy does not say how many firms it hopes to scale, what level of investment it seeks to attract, or what increased access to compute would look like in practice.
- **Gaps** -- The section leans heavily on the language of sovereign AI and competitiveness, but it does not confront the practical limits of Scottish autonomy in this space. Access to frontier compute, cloud infrastructure, semiconductor supply chains, and global capital markets is shaped by forces well beyond Scotland's control. The strategy would be stronger if it were more explicit about which parts of this agenda are realistically influenceable.
- **Feasibility** -- Some of the proposed actions are sensible, especially the needs assessment and efforts to improve compute access. But the leap from those interventions to a globally competitive AI sector is large. The section underplays how difficult it is to build and retain scalable AI companies outside the largest capital and infrastructure centres.
- **Internal consistency** -- Responsible innovation is presented as a competitive strength, which fits the strategy's broader ethical positioning. That is coherent in principle, but the section still leaves unresolved how the government will balance support for rapid company growth with the safeguards and trust commitments emphasised elsewhere in the document.
