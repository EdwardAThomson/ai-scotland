# Review: Layer 8 - Regulation

## Summary

This section frames regulation as a source of certainty, trust, and market access rather than simply a constraint on innovation. It argues that Scotland should align around OECD principles, seek closer compatibility with the EU AI Act, and influence UK-level regulation where that is necessary to protect Scottish interests.

## Key Points

- Regulation is presented as essential for public confidence, investor certainty, and responsible AI deployment.
- The 2031 outcomes focus on principles-based governance, a clear regulatory environment, market access, and international engagement.
- The strategy reaffirms the OECD AI principles as a core foundation for Scottish AI governance.
- It argues for closer alignment between Scottish and UK regulatory approaches and stronger footing for OECD principles in legislation or guidance.
- The section highlights sector-specific experimentation through regulatory sandboxes.
- It treats the EU AI Act as an important reference point and explicitly links regulatory alignment to access to European markets, investment, and opportunities.
- The 2027 actions focus on advocacy, review, reporting, and potential additional safeguards in devolved areas.

## Observations

- **Clarity** -- The section is relatively clear about its strategic preference: regulation should support trust and market access, and Scotland should push for alignment with higher-standard international regimes where possible. That makes the overall direction easier to understand than in some earlier chapters.
- **Measurability** -- The actions are mostly procedural. They commit the government to advocate, review, and publish reports, but they say little about what concrete regulatory change will follow or how success in `alignment` will be judged. This makes the section more of a positioning statement than a delivery plan.
- **Gaps** -- The main gap is practical agency. Much of the relevant regulatory framework sits at UK level or beyond, yet the section does not fully spell out what Scotland can change directly in devolved areas and what it can only attempt to influence. It also says relatively little about enforcement capacity, institutional capability, or how regulatory clarity will reach smaller organisations.
- **Feasibility** -- The section is realistic to the extent that it acknowledges shared competence and the importance of UK-wide coordination. But that realism also limits the power of its promises. The government can credibly advocate and review, yet the document is less convincing on how much of the desired regulatory environment it can actually deliver.
- **Internal consistency** -- This chapter is broadly consistent with the strategy's repeated emphasis on responsible AI and public trust. It also connects well with the companies and data layers. The tension is that the strategy often presents Scotland as a distinct ethical leader while depending heavily on external regulatory systems to give that leadership practical effect.
