# Review: Layer 5 - Data Centres and Infrastructure

## Summary

This section treats digital infrastructure as a strategic foundation for the wider AI ecosystem, linking data centres and compute capacity to sovereignty, skills, innovation, and national resilience. It also tries to align that growth agenda with sustainability concerns around energy, water, heat reuse, and local benefit.

## Key Points

- Hosting data centres and advanced compute infrastructure is presented as strategically important for AI capability and economic growth.
- The 2031 outcomes focus on sustainable investment, grid connectivity, local benefits, and resilient sovereign infrastructure.
- The strategy identifies water stewardship, grid connection reform, investor confidence, and distributed compute deployment as key infrastructure challenges.
- It recognises that some important levers, especially around grid access, sit with the UK Government and system operator rather than Scotland alone.
- The 2027 actions include support for the Lanarkshire AI Growth Zone, exploration of an AI accelerator, promotion of investment opportunities, heat offtake work, and guidance on what counts as a green data centre.
- The Isle of Eigg case study is used to illustrate community-led renewable infrastructure and local resilience.

## Observations

- **Clarity** -- The section is clearer than many growth-oriented parts of the strategy because it acknowledges real infrastructure constraints such as water use, grid access, and planning conditions. It does not pretend that renewable generation alone solves the problem of AI-scale infrastructure.
- **Measurability** -- The outcomes remain broad and partly reputational. Phrases such as `recognised as a leader` and `benefits realised` are not enough on their own. The section does not define how community benefit will be assessed, what connectivity improvement is needed, or how much compute capacity Scotland is aiming to secure.
- **Gaps** -- The biggest gap is trade-off management. The section says choices about infrastructure matter, but it does not set out how Scotland will balance hyperscale investment against local environmental pressures, land use, energy allocation, or community consent. It also leaves vague what `sovereign` capability actually means in a context dominated by global cloud providers and UK-wide infrastructure governance.
- **Feasibility** -- The document is reasonably realistic in noting that key levers are reserved to the UK, but that also exposes how constrained Scottish control is over the agenda. The proposed actions may help improve the investment environment, yet the leap from those actions to a resilient sovereign infrastructure base is substantial.
- **Internal consistency** -- This section aligns with the strategy's attempt to couple AI growth with sustainability and public value. Still, there is a visible tension between the ambition to attract large-scale infrastructure and the promise that development will be inclusive, community-benefiting, and environmentally responsible. The strategy recognises that tension but does not fully resolve it.
