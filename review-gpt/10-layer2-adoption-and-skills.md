# Review: Layer 2 - Adoption and Skills

## Summary

This section argues that AI adoption is not just about technology uptake but about leadership, organisational readiness, workforce capability, and Fair Work. It combines an economic case for adoption with a skills and labour-market argument, making it one of the more operationally grounded parts of the strategy.

## Key Points

- Successful AI adoption is defined as an organisational capability issue, not simply a technical deployment choice.
- The section identifies low AI adoption among Scottish SMEs and a shortage of relevant skills as major barriers.
- It links responsible adoption to public trust, especially in public and third sector settings.
- Fair Work is explicitly invoked as a principle for managing workforce change caused by AI.
- The existing SME AI Adoption Programme is presented as an early success, with engagement figures, leadership development activity, and hands-on support for firms.
- By 2027, the government plans a revitalised adoption programme, an AI Leadership Academy, a standardised readiness tool, modular literacy training, and a Future Jobs Panel.
- The 2031 outcomes focus on effective adoption, workforce skills, skills aligned to need, and responsible AI in public services.

## Observations

- **Clarity** -- The section is relatively strong because it treats adoption as a socio-technical problem involving leadership, skills, governance, and worker engagement. That gives it more practical substance than sections that rely mainly on promotional language.
- **Measurability** -- This section includes some useful programme numbers from the existing SME adoption work, which helps anchor the narrative. However, the future outcomes still stop short of saying how much adoption should rise, how skills shortages will be measured, or what improved job quality under Fair Work would concretely look like.
- **Gaps** -- The section correctly identifies that workforce change matters, but it is still vague about labour-market risk. It does not specify which occupations or sectors are most exposed, how worker voice will be built into adoption decisions, or what protections exist if AI adoption degrades job quality rather than improving it.
- **Feasibility** -- The proposed interventions are credible and more concrete than many elsewhere in the strategy, especially because they build on an existing programme rather than starting from zero. The harder question is whether these interventions are large enough to shift adoption behaviour across the whole Scottish economy rather than helping a relatively small number of firms at the margin.
- **Internal consistency** -- This section is one of the few places where Fair Work is integrated meaningfully rather than mentioned in passing. That makes it more consistent with the foreword than many of the surrounding chapters. Even so, the strategy still needs to show that Fair Work will shape implementation decisions, not just the language used to describe them.
