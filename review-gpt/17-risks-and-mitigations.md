# Review: Risks and Mitigations

## Summary

This section acknowledges that AI growth creates significant risks and that not all of them are yet fully known. It identifies a small set of headline risks and pairs them with high-level mitigation approaches, while also promising that future impact assessments will examine equality, human rights, privacy, environmental sustainability, and related concerns.

## Key Points

- The strategy recognises that AI risks will evolve over time and cannot be fully specified in advance.
- The government says it will work with stakeholders including trade unions, community groups, industry, academia, and the public sector to understand emerging impacts.
- Impact assessments are promised as policy and implementation plans are developed.
- The risk table covers privacy and data protection, workforce impacts, environmental impacts, renewable energy capacity, gender inequality, sovereign infrastructure, and sector-specific disruption.
- Mitigations are mostly framed through broader strategy themes such as cyber resilience, Fair Work, sustainable infrastructure, equality considerations, and domestic capability-building.

## Observations

- **Clarity** -- The section is straightforward and usefully concise, and it is helpful that it explicitly admits uncertainty. That is more credible than pretending the full risk landscape is already known.
- **Measurability** -- The mitigations are not very measurable. Most are framed as broad policy directions rather than specific controls, thresholds, or accountability mechanisms. That makes it hard to tell whether the identified risks are being reduced in practice or simply acknowledged rhetorically.
- **Gaps** -- Some important risks receive only limited treatment. The section does not say much about bias beyond gender inequality, nor does it address misinformation, concentration of market power, procurement failure, exclusion of disabled users, or legal redress for harmful decisions. The table is useful, but it feels selective rather than comprehensive.
- **Feasibility** -- The commitment to future impact assessments is sensible, but it pushes much of the real mitigation work downstream into later implementation. That may be unavoidable, yet it also means the section offers less immediate assurance than its framing might suggest.
- **Internal consistency** -- The risks map reasonably well onto themes raised elsewhere in the strategy, especially Fair Work, sustainability, data governance, and infrastructure. At the same time, the brevity of the mitigations highlights a wider pattern across the document: the strategy is generally better at naming principles and areas of concern than at specifying the mechanisms that will manage them.
