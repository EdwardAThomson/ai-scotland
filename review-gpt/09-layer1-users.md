# Review: Layer 1 - Users

## Summary

This section focuses on the public-facing experience of AI, especially as AI becomes more embedded in public services. It argues that trust, transparency, literacy, and inclusive service design are necessary conditions for legitimate adoption, and it frames citizens not just as service users but as people whose rights and confidence must be protected.

## Key Points

- AI is increasingly shaping citizens' interactions with public services, especially in health and social care.
- The strategy links this layer to Scotland's wider digital inclusion and public-service reform agenda.
- Public confidence in AI is said to depend on literacy, clear communication, and understanding of rights and safeguards.
- A human and user-centred service design approach is presented as essential.
- The 2031 outcomes focus on informed citizens, inclusive AI literacy, prepared young people, and trusted public-service use of AI.
- The 2027 actions include public engagement, health and social care assurance work, literacy materials, and greater visibility of AI use across the public sector.
- The Citizens Advice Scotland case study is used to illustrate AI supporting human judgement in safeguarding rather than replacing it.

## Observations

- **Clarity** -- The section is clearer than some others because it defines a recognisable problem: if people do not understand or trust AI in public services, adoption will be fragile and potentially unjust. It makes a persuasive case that user experience and legitimacy are not secondary issues.
- **Measurability** -- The outcomes are still broad. Terms like `informed`, `inclusive`, and `trusted` are directionally sensible but are not tied to survey measures, transparency standards, participation rates, or other indicators that would let progress be assessed properly by 2031.
- **Gaps** -- The section talks about rights, fairness, and transparency, but it remains light on redress. It does not explain what citizens can do if they are harmed by an AI-enabled decision, how explanation rights will work in practice, or how people will challenge the use of AI in high-stakes settings.
- **Feasibility** -- The proposed actions are plausible as a first phase, but building genuine public trust is harder than publishing materials and running engagement exercises. Trust will depend heavily on the quality of governance, disclosure, and real-world outcomes in public services, much of which sits beyond this layer alone.
- **Internal consistency** -- This is one of the sections that most directly reflects the strategy's wider language about responsible and inclusive AI. It also exposes a recurring tension in the document: strong rhetorical emphasis on trust and fairness, but limited detail on the concrete enforcement and accountability mechanisms that would make those commitments credible.
