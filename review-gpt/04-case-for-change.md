# Review: Case for Change

## Summary

This section makes the clearest argument yet for intervention. It says Scotland must act now because AI presents a large economic opportunity, adoption is still uneven, public-service demand is rising, data infrastructure is fragmented, and international competition and standards are moving quickly.

## Key Points

- AI is projected to offer substantial economic value, including a claimed additional `£23 billion` in annual GDP by 2035.
- Business adoption remains limited, with only `30.7%` of businesses reported as currently using AI.
- Public services face rising demand and constrained finances, and AI is presented as a tool for improving planning, prevention, and frontline support.
- Public-sector data is described as fragmented and hard to access, making better data governance and sharing a priority.
- International standards such as the OECD AI Principles and the EU AI Act are presented as shaping the operating environment.
- AI is said to place new demands on energy and other critical infrastructure.
- Scotland's research base, start-up ecosystem, and skills pipeline all require further support if the country is to capture the opportunity.

## Observations

- **Clarity** -- This is one of the more grounded sections in the document. It identifies several concrete pressures at once: adoption gaps, data fragmentation, infrastructure demands, skills shortages, and geopolitical competition.
- **Measurability** -- The section uses more quantified claims than earlier parts of the strategy, but the most prominent figures still need scrutiny. The GDP headline is conditional on `the right investment and leadership`, which makes it less a forecast than a scenario. The adoption figure is more useful, but it would be stronger if benchmarked against the UK average or peer countries.
- **Gaps** -- The section does not say enough about cost, trade-offs, or sequencing. It argues that Scotland must act, but it does not indicate what level of public investment, institutional reform, or political prioritisation will actually be required. It also says choices will need to be made about energy allocation without confronting what those choices might displace.
- **Feasibility** -- The diagnosis is more credible than some of the promotional material elsewhere, but the implied response is still very ambitious for a nation operating within limited devolved powers and intense global competition. The argument would be stronger if it distinguished more clearly between what Scotland can control directly and what depends on UK-wide or international conditions.
- **Internal consistency** -- This section improves the overall coherence of the strategy because it names problems that later chapters ought to solve. It creates a useful test for the action plan: whether the subsequent sections actually respond to low adoption, data fragmentation, infrastructure pressure, and skills shortages in a specific enough way.
