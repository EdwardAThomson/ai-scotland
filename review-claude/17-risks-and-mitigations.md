# Review: Risks and Mitigations

## Summary

The final substantive section identifies seven risks associated with AI growth and maps each to a mitigating outcome from the strategy. It acknowledges that not all risks are known and commits to impact assessments as policies are developed. The risks cover privacy, workforce, environment, energy, gender inequality, sovereign infrastructure, and sector-specific disruption.

## Key Points

- Seven risks identified: privacy and data protection, workforce impacts, environmental impacts, renewable energy capacity, gender inequality, sovereign infrastructure, sector-specific impacts.
- Each risk is paired with a mitigating outcome from elsewhere in the strategy.
- The Scottish Government commits to ongoing stakeholder engagement (including trade unions and community groups) to explore emerging risks.
- Impact assessments will be conducted on equality, human rights, privacy, environmental sustainability, and other considerations as policies are developed.

## Observations

- **Clarity** -- The table format is clear and easy to read. However, the "mitigating outcomes" column describes desired end states, not actual mitigations. For example, the mitigation for workforce impacts is "quality jobs and future capability are supported through accessible reskilling" -- this is an aspiration, not a programme, policy, or action. A mitigation should describe what will be done to reduce the risk, not what the world looks like if the risk is successfully managed.
- **Measurability** -- None of the mitigations are measurable. How would you know if "energy efficiency in data centres is improved"? Against what baseline? By how much? The section functions as a risk register without risk scores, owners, timelines, or monitoring mechanisms.
- **Gaps**:
  - **Major risks are missing.** Misinformation and deepfakes, algorithmic bias beyond gender (race, disability, age, socioeconomic status), cybersecurity threats from AI-enabled attacks, democratic integrity, and concentration of power in a small number of AI companies are all globally recognised AI risks that do not appear here.
  - **No risk related to the strategy itself failing.** What if adoption does not reach expected levels? What if the £23 billion GDP projection does not materialise? What if AI Scotland is not effective? The strategy does not consider its own execution risks.
  - **"Gender Inequality" is the only named equality risk.** The Equality Act 2010 covers nine protected characteristics. Limiting the discussion to gender is incomplete and inconsistent with the commitment to impact assessments covering "equality, human rights, privacy, environmental sustainability and other relevant considerations."
  - **No mention of children and young people.** AI's impact on children (in education, social media, data collection, safeguarding) is a major policy area globally. Its absence is notable.
  - **Trade unions are mentioned** for the first time in a substantive context (stakeholder engagement on emerging risks). This is welcome but late -- they are not embedded in any delivery mechanism.
- **Feasibility** -- The commitment to impact assessments "as policies and implementation plans are developed" is prudent. However, it also means that the detailed risk analysis is deferred to a future stage. At the point of publication, the strategy has identified seven headline risks but has not yet assessed their likelihood, severity, or interdependencies.
- **Internal consistency** -- The mitigating outcomes reference commitments from across the strategy, which shows some attempt at integration. However, many of the referenced outcomes were themselves found to be vague and unmeasurable in earlier sections. A mitigation that points to an unmeasurable outcome does not reduce risk -- it creates an illusion of coverage. This section would be significantly strengthened by linking risks to specific actions rather than to broad outcomes.
