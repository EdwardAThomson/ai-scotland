# Review: Layer 7 - Data

## Summary

This layer addresses the fragmented state of Scotland's public sector data and the need to make it accessible, AI-ready, and securely shareable. It acknowledges that despite strong governance frameworks, the full value of public data as a national asset is not being realised. Four actions target a data matchmaking pilot, barrier identification, a public services innovation programme, and a joint leadership group with local government. The case study covers NPL's AI trustworthiness framework at the University of Strathclyde.

## Key Points

- Public sector data is fragmented across systems and organisations; access is slow and complex.
- Other nations are accelerating investment in data infrastructure, creating a competitive risk.
- Four 2027 actions: data matchmaking pilot, barrier/enabler assessment for public data access, public services AI innovation programme, joint data leadership group with local government.
- 2031 outcomes target a "mature and coordinated" public sector data ecosystem with AI-ready data.
- Case study: NPL's Data Science & AI Hub at Strathclyde, developing a trustworthiness and safety framework for AI systems.

## Observations

- **Clarity** -- The problem statement is clear and honest: data is fragmented, access is slow, and Scotland risks falling behind. This is one of the strategy's most straightforward diagnoses. However, the solutions are generic. "Work with public sector organisations to identify the barriers" is an action to understand the problem, not to solve it. At the time of publication, the government evidently does not know what the specific barriers are.
- **Measurability** -- No metrics for any outcome. "Easy to access" data, "mature" ecosystem, "highest levels of security" -- none of these can be assessed without definitions and baselines. How many datasets should be available through the data matchmaking pilot? How many organisations should be sharing data by 2031? What does "AI-ready" mean technically?
- **Gaps**:
  - **Private sector data is entirely absent.** The section focuses exclusively on public sector data. Scotland's AI companies also need access to commercial datasets, synthetic data, and industry benchmarks. The data matchmaking pilot could include private sector participants, but this is not stated.
  - **Technical detail is missing.** The section does not address data standards, interoperability formats, metadata requirements, or the technical infrastructure needed to make data shareable. "Modernise secure data infrastructure" is stated as a need but no approach is described.
  - **Research Data Scotland is not mentioned.** This existing body was specifically created to enable secure access to Scotland's public sector data for research. Its absence from a section about public data access is a surprising omission (though it appears in the endnotes).
  - **Data ethics and privacy are thin.** The section references "transparency, safety and public good" but does not engage with the specific privacy challenges of making public data AI-ready -- de-identification, re-identification risks, consent models, or the role of the Information Commissioner.
- **Feasibility** -- The joint leadership group with local government is a pragmatic action -- local authorities hold vast amounts of data (housing, planning, social care, education) and their cooperation is essential. However, Scotland has 32 local authorities with varying digital maturity. Coordinating data sharing across this landscape is a multi-year challenge that four actions barely begin to address.
- **Internal consistency** -- The public services innovation programme (action 3) appears for the third time in the strategy, also featuring in the ten key actions (section 06, action 8) and Layer 4 (section 12). This repetition inflates the apparent volume of actions. The NPL case study is relevant to data trust and governance but is a UK-wide initiative rather than a distinctly Scottish one.
