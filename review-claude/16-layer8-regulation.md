# Review: Layer 8 - Regulation

## Summary

The Regulation layer navigates the complex relationship between Scotland's devolved powers, UK-level regulation, and the EU AI Act. The Scottish Government positions itself as an advocate for OECD-aligned, principles-based regulation at the UK level, while signalling a preference for closer alignment with the EU AI Act than the UK Government has pursued. Actions focus on advocacy, review, and reporting rather than direct regulatory action.

## Key Points

- Regulation is largely reserved to the UK Government; Scotland's role is primarily advocacy and influence.
- The strategy reaffirms commitment to OECD AI principles as a "chain of trust."
- The Scottish Government advocates for UK regulation that enables alignment with the EU AI Act, citing market access and the Scottish Government's established pro-European position.
- The EU AI Act's statutory framework creates a compliance burden for actors in non-statutory regimes (i.e. the UK), which disadvantages Scottish businesses accessing European markets.
- Regulatory sandboxes are endorsed as a mechanism for controlled testing.
- Four 2027 actions: advocate for OECD principles on a statutory footing, review devolved regulatory scope, explore EU AI Act alignment, publish a regulation report.

## Observations

- **Clarity** -- This is the most politically significant section. The Scottish Government is explicitly stating that the UK's current approach to AI regulation is insufficient and that closer EU alignment is needed. The argument is well constructed: the EU AI Act's statutory obligations create asymmetric compliance burdens for businesses in non-statutory regimes. However, the section could be clearer about what Scotland can actually regulate. The boundary between reserved and devolved regulatory powers is not explained, leaving the reader uncertain about what the "review and report on scope" might find.
- **Measurability** -- All four actions are studies, reviews, or advocacy positions. None creates a regulatory change. By 2027, Scotland will have advocated, reviewed, and published a report -- but may not have changed anything. The 2031 outcomes assume "a clear regulatory framework" exists, but the strategy provides no mechanism to create one, because the power to do so largely rests with the UK Government.
- **Gaps**:
  - **What can Scotland actually regulate?** The devolved/reserved boundary is the central question but is left unanswered. Health, education, and justice are devolved; could Scotland regulate AI use in these sectors independently? The section promises a review but does not preview the answer.
  - **No mention of enforcement.** Even where Scotland has regulatory scope, enforcement requires institutional capacity. Who enforces AI regulation in Scotland -- existing regulators, a new body, or the UK Information Commissioner?
  - **Intellectual property is mentioned once** (in the opening sentence) and never addressed again. AI-generated content, model training on copyrighted data, and IP ownership are live issues with significant implications for Scotland's creative industries and research base.
  - **No timeline for potential Scottish legislation.** If the review finds scope for devolved regulation, how quickly could Scotland act? The action plan ends at 2027 with a report; legislative action would presumably be phases 2 or 3, but these are undefined.
- **Feasibility** -- The strategy's regulatory ambitions are constrained by constitutional reality. Scotland can advocate, review, and report, but cannot unilaterally create the "clear regulatory framework" its outcomes describe. This is the most honest section about the limits of devolved power, though it could be more explicit. The EU AI Act alignment aspiration is politically resonant but operationally difficult within a UK-wide regulatory framework.
- **Internal consistency** -- This section engages directly with the EU AI Act and OECD principles -- the only international frameworks referenced in the strategy. It connects well to the Case for Change (section 04), which flagged the global regulatory landscape. However, it does not reference the responsible AI commitments made in earlier sections (Fair Work, ethical AI, public trust) -- regulation is framed as a market access issue rather than a citizen protection issue.
