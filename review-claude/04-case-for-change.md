# Review: Case for Change

## Summary

This section argues that Scotland must act now on AI or risk falling behind. It cites a potential £23 billion annual GDP boost by 2035, flags low business adoption (30.7%), acknowledges fragmented public sector data, and frames the global environment as competitive and fast-moving. It is the most candid section so far about challenges Scotland faces.

## Key Points

- AI could generate an additional £23 billion in annual GDP by 2035 (cumulative £140.75 billion over 2025-2035), per GC Insight.
- Only 30.7% of Scottish businesses currently use AI, and many leaders lack confidence in adopting it safely.
- Public sector data is fragmented and difficult to access; improving data sharing and governance is essential.
- International standards (OECD AI principles, EU AI Act) are shaping the regulatory landscape and Scotland must remain aligned.
- AI places new demands on energy infrastructure; choices about power allocation are needed.
- Scotland's research base is strong but needs investment and global partnerships to translate into commercial outcomes.
- Skills shortages must be addressed at all levels.

## Observations

- **Clarity** -- This is the strongest section so far in terms of honesty. It acknowledges real problems: low adoption, fragmented data, skills shortages, and infrastructure pressure. The tone shifts from the promotional style of the Foundations section to something more analytical.
- **Measurability** -- The £23 billion GDP figure is the headline claim of the strategy but warrants scrutiny. The source (endnote 28) is a report commissioned by Scottish Enterprise from GC Insight that is not publicly available. "With the right investment and leadership" is a significant caveat -- it effectively means the figure is conditional on everything going to plan. The cumulative £140.75 billion figure sounds dramatic but is just the annual figure summed over 10 years, which is a presentational choice designed to impress rather than inform. The 30.7% adoption figure (endnote 30/36) is more useful and verifiable.
- **Gaps**:
  - **No comparison with other nations.** The section says the global environment is "volatile and competitive" and that "other countries are moving quickly" but never names them or quantifies the gap. How does Scotland's 30.7% adoption compare to the UK average, or to Ireland, Denmark, or Estonia?
  - **No cost estimate.** The section presents the prize (£23 billion GDP) but never estimates what it will cost to get there. What level of public and private investment is required to achieve these outcomes?
  - **"Skills shortages at all levels" is vague.** Which skills? At what scale? This is flagged as a problem but not quantified.
- **Feasibility** -- The section implicitly asks Scotland to compete in an "innovation race" against much larger economies without addressing the resource asymmetry. The acknowledgment that "choices need to be made about the allocation of power" for AI infrastructure is refreshingly honest but leaves the hard question unanswered: what gets deprioritised?
- **Internal consistency** -- This section is more balanced than Foundations and begins to set up the actions that follow. The acknowledgment of fragmented public data, low adoption, and skills gaps creates clear expectations for the action plan. The question is whether the subsequent sections deliver concrete responses to each of these challenges.
