# Review: Layer 5 - Data Centres and Infrastructure

## Summary

The longest layer section (5 pages) covers data centre investment, grid connectivity, water infrastructure, distributed compute, and the "Scottish Green Compute" proposition. It positions Scotland's renewable energy as a competitive advantage for attracting hyperscale and distributed data centre investment. Actions focus on the Lanarkshire AI Growth Zone, investor pipeline, heat reuse, and green data centre guidance. The case study is about the Isle of Eigg's community renewable energy, not data centres or AI.

## Key Points

- Data centres are framed as critical national infrastructure and a matter of sovereignty.
- Scotland's renewable energy and water resources are positioned as competitive advantages.
- Grid connection timescales are acknowledged as a bottleneck; this is a UK-reserved policy area.
- Scottish Water is advising on sustainable water use for data centres.
- "Green AI-Ready" zones are proposed, subject to local authority planning decisions.
- Distributed compute (smaller, geographically spread facilities) is highlighted alongside hyperscale.
- Five 2027 actions: Lanarkshire AI Growth Zone delivery, AI Accelerator for the zone, investor pipeline, heat offtake opportunities, green data centre guidance.
- Case study: Isle of Eigg -- world's first community to run entirely on renewables.

## Observations

- **Clarity** -- The section is well structured and addresses real infrastructure challenges (grid connectivity, water, distributed compute). The "Scottish Green Compute" proposition is a clear brand concept. However, the distinction between what Scotland can control and what is reserved to the UK Government could be clearer -- grid infrastructure and energy policy are significantly constrained by UK-level decisions, and the strategy's ability to deliver on connectivity outcomes depends on a partner over which it has limited influence.
- **Measurability** -- No targets for data centre capacity, compute power, jobs created, or investment attracted. How many data centres should Scotland have by 2031? What compute capacity? The section is entirely qualitative. "Publish guidance on what factors are prevalent in a 'green' data centre" is measurable but is a documentation exercise, not an infrastructure outcome.
- **Gaps**:
  - **Environmental impact is understated.** The section acknowledges water and energy demands but frames them primarily as logistical challenges to solve rather than genuine tensions. Large-scale data centres consume significant energy that could otherwise power homes or industry. The strategy does not address the trade-off: every megawatt allocated to a data centre is a megawatt not available for something else.
  - **Community impact beyond heat reuse is absent.** Data centres bring jobs during construction but relatively few permanent roles. The outcome says "tangible benefits for local communities" but the only mechanism identified is heat offtake. What about noise, landscape impact, or competition for resources?
  - **Cost and public investment are unmentioned.** The £15 billion North Ayrshire and £2.5 billion Lanarkshire projects (from Foundations) are private investments. What public funding is required to support grid upgrades, planning processes, or water infrastructure? The strategy is silent.
  - **Latency and connectivity to international markets.** Scotland's geographic position creates latency challenges for real-time AI workloads compared to data centres in London, Amsterdam, or Frankfurt. This is not discussed.
- **Feasibility** -- The grid connection bottleneck is the critical constraint, and the section honestly acknowledges it while also admitting it is outside Scotland's direct control. This is a significant risk to the entire layer. The NESO Grid Connection Reform Process is welcomed but its timeline and outcomes are uncertain.
- **Internal consistency** -- The case study is the strategy's weakest match to its section. The Isle of Eigg is inspiring but is a 100-person community generating 280 kW of power. A single hyperscale data centre requires 100-500+ MW. The case study demonstrates community-led renewables, not AI infrastructure capability. It feels included to celebrate a Scottish success story rather than to illustrate the section's argument.
