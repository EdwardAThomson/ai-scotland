# Review: Layer 4 - Innovation, Research and Development

## Summary

This layer tackles the acknowledged gap between Scotland's strong AI research base and its weak commercialisation record. It proposes a national cluster scheme, a Venture Creator pilot for university commercialisation, and a public services innovation programme. The Chemify case study -- a University of Glasgow spin-out that raised $50 million and created 180 jobs -- is the strategy's strongest example of research-to-commercial translation.

## Key Points

- Scotland's research is internationally strong but is "not converting into economic impact at the scale it should."
- The problem is commercialisation, not research quality.
- Five 2027 actions: public services innovation programme, national cluster scheme, cluster financial support, Venture Creator pilot for university commercialisation, progressing the Scottish Spin-out Report.
- 2031 outcomes: world-leading research, commercial impact from research, cluster development, public-sector-driven innovation.
- Case study: Chemify reduced development timelines from months to days, raised $50 million Series B, created 180+ jobs, opened a £12 million facility.

## Observations

- **Clarity** -- The opening statement is the most honest in the strategy: Scotland's research excellence isn't translating into economic impact. This is refreshingly direct and sets up the section well. The "Venture Creator" concept is interesting but only loosely defined -- it is described as "bringing together all the essential elements of commercialisation" without specifying what those elements are or how it differs from existing technology transfer offices.
- **Measurability** -- No targets for any action. How many spin-outs should the Venture Creator produce? How many clusters should be established? What does "globally recognised" mean for cluster development? The Chemify case study demonstrates what success looks like ($50 million raised, 180 jobs, £12 million facility) but the strategy does not set comparable benchmarks for its own programmes.
- **Gaps**:
  - **No diagnosis of why commercialisation fails.** The section says the problem exists but doesn't analyse causes. Is it risk aversion in universities? Lack of VC funding? IP policies that discourage spin-outs? Talent leaving for London? Without a diagnosis, the proposed interventions (cluster scheme, Venture Creator) may not address the actual barriers.
  - **No mention of the funding gap.** University spin-outs typically struggle at the seed and Series A stages. The section proposes a Venture Creator but does not mention funding vehicles, co-investment programmes, or how to close the gap between proof of concept and commercial viability.
  - **International partnerships are mentioned but not specified.** The section calls for "global research partnerships" and "appropriate academic and business partnerships across the globe" but names no target countries, institutions, or mechanisms.
  - **Colleges are mentioned in the outcomes but not in the actions.** The outcome says "universities and colleges" but every action focuses on universities. College-based innovation and applied research are absent from the delivery plan.
- **Feasibility** -- The Venture Creator is described as a pilot, which is appropriate for a new model. The national cluster scheme is more ambitious but "establish a national cluster scheme" is a structural intervention that typically takes years to bear fruit. Expecting globally recognised clusters by 2031 from a scheme established in 2027 is optimistic.
- **Internal consistency** -- This section directly addresses the commercialisation gap identified in the Case for Change (section 04), which is a strength. The Chemify case study is an excellent match for the section's argument. However, the public services innovation action overlaps with Layer 1 (Users) and the ten key actions (action 8), creating redundancy across the strategy.
