# Review: AI Action Plan

## Summary

This section introduces the AI Stack -- the strategy's organising framework -- as an eight-layer model covering Users, Adoption & Skills, Companies & Products, Innovation & R&D, Data Centres & Infrastructure, and Semiconductors, with Data and Regulation as cross-cutting layers. The action plan will be delivered in three phases (this document covers phase 1), with updates in 2027 and 2029.

## Key Points

- The AI Stack is described as a "radical new approach" to organising government response to AI.
- Six core layers arranged vertically (Users at top, Semiconductors at bottom), plus Data and Regulation as encircling layers.
- Layer positions are explicitly stated to be non-hierarchical.
- The action plan is phased: phase 1 (this strategy), phase 2 (2027), phase 3 (2029), targeting 2031 outcomes.
- Closing statement: "If we deliver our actions and outcomes, we will achieve our purpose."

## Observations

- **Clarity** -- Describing the AI Stack as "radical" overstates its novelty. Layered models for technology ecosystems are well-established (the OSI model, technology stack diagrams, etc.). What is useful here is adapting the concept for policy purposes. The claim that layers are "non-hierarchical" while being presented in a vertical stack is contradictory -- the visual framing inevitably implies hierarchy. The Data and Regulation layers being "encircling" is an interesting design choice but may confuse readers who expect a simple list.
- **Measurability** -- The phased delivery model (2026, 2027, 2029) is a strength. It builds in review points and allows course correction. However, the closing line -- "If we deliver our actions and outcomes, we will achieve our purpose" -- is circular. The purpose is defined so broadly ("responsible and inclusive growth... positive difference at every level of society") that any set of delivered actions could be claimed to achieve it.
- **Gaps**:
  - **No explanation of why these layers.** Why six core layers plus two cross-cutting? Why not include, for example, a layer for ethics, public trust, or international partnerships? The framework is presented as given rather than justified.
  - **No interaction model.** The section says layers "interact with all other layers" but does not describe how. Which dependencies are critical? What happens if one layer lags behind? A framework's value lies in explaining relationships, not just naming components.
  - **Phase 2 and phase 3 are blank.** The strategy acknowledges this is only phase 1, but provides no indication of what phases 2 and 3 might address. This means the strategy is really a 12-month action plan branded as a five-year strategy.
- **Internal consistency** -- The AI Stack's six core layers do not map cleanly to the four outcome groupings from section 05 (People & Skills, Companies & Innovation, Infrastructure, Data & Regulation). Users (Layer 1) has no obvious outcome; Semiconductors (Layer 6) has no dedicated outcome. The structural mismatch between the strategy's outcomes framework and its delivery framework is a weakness -- it makes traceability from actions to outcomes harder.
