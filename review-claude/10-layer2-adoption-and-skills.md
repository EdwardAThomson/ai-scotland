# Review: Layer 2 - Adoption and Skills

## Summary

The most substantive layer section so far. It addresses both business AI adoption and workforce skills, anchored by concrete data on current adoption rates and a detailed account of the existing £1 million SME AI Adoption Programme. Five actions target 2027, including a revitalised adoption programme, an AI Leadership Academy, and a Future Jobs Panel. The EGG Lighting case study demonstrates tangible SME benefits.

## Key Points

- 61.9% of Scottish SMEs are not using AI (BICS survey, March 2025).
- 5,700 job postings required at least one AI skill between July 2024 and June 2025.
- The existing £1 million SME AI Adoption Programme engaged 500+ SMEs, scoped 80+ early use cases, trained 120+ senior leaders, and provided hands-on support to 160+ companies.
- Five 2027 actions: revitalised adoption programme, AI Leadership Academy, AI readiness tool, modular AI literacy training, Future Jobs Panel.
- Fair Work is explicitly mentioned as a guiding principle for workforce transition.
- Case study: EGG Lighting achieved £40k-£60k cost avoidance, 60% cut in content preparation time, and 30-40 minutes saved per delivery through AI adoption.

## Observations

- **Clarity** -- This is the clearest and most grounded layer section. The opening paragraph's definition of "successful adoption" is sensible -- it acknowledges that not every business needs wholesale AI integration. The existing programme's results are presented with specific numbers, and the case study is compelling.
- **Measurability** -- The existing programme provides a useful baseline: 500 SMEs engaged out of ~360,000 in Scotland. This is 0.14% of the SME base. The section does not state what the revitalised programme aims to achieve -- how many SMEs should be reached by 2027 or 2031? Without a target, it is impossible to judge whether the programme is scaling fast enough. The EGG Lighting case study, by contrast, is a model of measurability.
- **Gaps**:
  - **Higher education and further education are absent.** The section addresses workplace skills and SME leadership but says nothing about university AI programmes, college courses, or the academic pipeline feeding into the workforce. Skills Development Scotland and the Scottish Funding Council are not mentioned.
  - **Large enterprises are not addressed.** The focus is entirely on SMEs. What about Scotland's large employers -- financial services firms, NHS, local authorities, energy companies? Their adoption challenges and workforce impacts are different in kind.
  - **The Future Jobs Panel is undefined.** Who sits on it? What is its remit, budget, or relationship to existing bodies like the Fair Work Convention? "Establish a panel" is the action -- what the panel actually does is deferred.
  - **No mention of job displacement.** The section frames AI as a skills and adoption opportunity but does not address the possibility that AI will eliminate roles. The Fair Work reference is welcome but vague -- there are no commitments on retraining support, transitional income, or redundancy protections.
- **Feasibility** -- The £1 million programme is a promising pilot but is very small relative to the challenge. If the revitalised programme has a similar budget, reaching a meaningful share of Scotland's SMEs will take decades. The section does not indicate whether significantly more funding is planned.
- **Internal consistency** -- This is the first layer section to explicitly reference Fair Work, delivering on the foreword's commitment. The connection to the Digital Public Services Delivery Plan and the Scottish AI Register also shows cross-referencing to existing policy. However, the 2031 outcomes repeat verbatim from the top of the section with no additional detail -- they are not refined by the intervening analysis.
