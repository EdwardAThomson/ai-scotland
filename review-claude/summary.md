# Claude Review: Summary of Findings

A summary of the Claude review of Scotland's AI Strategy 2026-2031, conducted section by section using the methodology defined in `review-methodology.md`. Individual section reviews are in `review-claude/`.

## Overall Assessment

Scotland's AI Strategy is a professionally written document that establishes a credible framework for thinking about AI policy. It correctly identifies Scotland's genuine strengths (research, renewables, niche semiconductor capability) and some of its real challenges (low business adoption, fragmented public data, commercialisation gaps). The AI Stack model is a useful organising structure, and several case studies effectively demonstrate responsible AI adoption in practice.

However, the strategy has significant structural weaknesses that undermine its effectiveness as a policy instrument. It reads more as a statement of ambition than a plan for delivery. The gap between what the strategy aspires to and what it commits to is wide.

## Key Findings

### 1. No measurable targets

This is the strategy's most fundamental weakness. None of the eight high-level outcomes, and none of the layer-specific outcomes, include a target, threshold, metric, or baseline. Phrases like "widespread literacy," "globally competitive," and "world-leading research" cannot be assessed. The government could claim success on every outcome in 2031 without demonstrating meaningful change.

The case studies demonstrate that measurable outcomes are achievable -- Citizens Advice Scotland's 35,000 cases, EGG Lighting's £40-60k cost avoidance, Chemify's $50 million raise. The strategy's own actions should aspire to the same specificity.

### 2. Actions create machinery, not results

The majority of the strategy's actions establish structures: panels, boards, academies, pilots, reviews, and reports. Very few commit to delivering a specific, tangible outcome. By March 2027, Scotland will have launched programmes and constituted advisory bodies, but the strategy does not specify what those programmes should achieve.

For example:
- "Establish a Future Jobs Panel" -- but no terms of reference, deliverables, or impact targets.
- "Pilot an AI Scale-up Accelerator" -- but no target for companies supported or funding raised.
- "Publish guidance on green data centres" -- a documentation exercise, not an infrastructure outcome.

### 3. No budgets or resource commitments

Not a single action in the strategy includes a cost, funding source, or resource allocation. The only figure attached to any programme is "nearly £1 million" for the existing SME AI Adoption Programme -- which reached 0.14% of Scotland's SMEs. An AI Leadership Academy, a Scale-up Accelerator, a nationwide engagement programme, and a data matchmaking pilot all require significant funding. Where it comes from is never addressed.

### 4. Phases 2 and 3 are empty

The strategy is presented as a five-year plan (2026-2031) delivered in three phases. But only phase 1 actions are defined. Phases 2 (2027) and 3 (2029) are blank. This means the strategy is effectively a 12-month action plan in a five-year wrapper. The long-term outcomes have no delivery pathway beyond the initial set of actions.

### 5. Fair Work is a phantom commitment

The foreword positions Fair Work as central to Scotland's AI approach. But this commitment is not embedded in delivery structures, governance, or actions. AI Scotland's consortium partners are tech-sector and enterprise-focused with no trade union representation. The Expert Advisory Board's membership criteria do not include worker representatives. Trade unions are mentioned once in a substantive context (risks and mitigations stakeholder engagement) and are otherwise absent.

### 6. Action repetition inflates apparent volume

Several actions appear multiple times across sections:
- The public services innovation programme appears in the ten key actions (section 06), Layer 4 (section 12), and Layer 7 (section 15).
- The Expert Advisory Board appears in the ten key actions and Layer 1 (section 09).
- The nationwide engagement programme appears in the ten key actions and Layer 1.

This creates an impression of more activity than the strategy actually proposes.

### 7. Incomplete risk register

The risks and mitigations section identifies seven risks but omits several globally recognised AI risks: misinformation and deepfakes, algorithmic bias beyond gender (race, disability, age), cybersecurity threats from AI-enabled attacks, democratic integrity, concentration of power in a small number of AI companies, and risks to children and young people. Gender inequality is the only named equality risk, despite the Equality Act covering nine protected characteristics. Mitigations describe aspirations rather than actions -- they point to outcomes that are themselves unmeasurable.

### 8. Key gaps across the strategy

Several important topics are absent or underserved:

| Gap | Detail |
|-----|--------|
| **Education** | No action addresses AI in schools, colleges, or university curricula. "Prepared young people" is an outcome with no delivery mechanism. |
| **Large enterprises** | The strategy focuses on SMEs. Scotland's large employers face different AI adoption challenges that are not addressed. |
| **Acquisition risk** | Successful Scottish AI companies are frequently acquired and relocated. This primary threat to the ecosystem is not discussed. |
| **International benchmarks** | The strategy rarely compares Scotland to peer nations. Statistics are presented without context. The semiconductors section is the only one to name comparator countries. |
| **Environmental trade-offs** | Data centres' energy and water demands are framed as logistical challenges, not genuine tensions with other uses. |
| **Intellectual property** | Mentioned once and never addressed. AI-generated content and training on copyrighted data are live issues. |
| **Right to challenge AI decisions** | Citizens should understand "what safeguards apply" but no complaints process, right to appeal, or human alternative is described. |
| **Accessibility** | For a strategy emphasising inclusive AI literacy, there is no mention of disabled users, non-English speakers, or those without internet access. |

## Strengths

The strategy is not without merit:

- **Honest problem statements.** The Case for Change, the Innovation & R&D layer, and the Data layer all acknowledge real problems clearly.
- **Semiconductor positioning.** The niche strategy for photonics and advanced packaging is realistic and well articulated -- the strongest strategic thinking in the document.
- **Case studies.** Citizens Advice Scotland, EGG Lighting, Chemify, and Quantcore are strong, specific examples of responsible AI adoption. They demonstrate what the strategy could look like if it were more concrete throughout.
- **Regulation section.** The EU AI Act analysis and the advocacy for OECD-aligned statutory regulation is the most politically significant and well-argued section.
- **Phased delivery model.** Building in review points (2027, 2029) is structurally sound, even though phases 2 and 3 are undefined.

## Conclusion

Scotland's AI Strategy establishes a reasonable framework and identifies real strengths and challenges. Its weakness is not in what it says but in what it commits to. Without measurable targets, budgets, and delivery mechanisms, the strategy risks becoming a document that describes the future Scotland wants without building the bridge to get there.

The quality of the case studies shows that Scottish organisations are already doing AI well. The strategy should hold itself to the same standard of specificity and measurability that its best examples demonstrate.
