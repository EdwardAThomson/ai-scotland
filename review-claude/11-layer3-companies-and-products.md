# Review: Layer 3 - Companies and Products

## Summary

This layer argues that Scotland must be a producer, not just a consumer, of AI, framing sovereign AI capability as a strategic necessity. It sets outcomes around a globally competitive AI sector, international investment, responsible innovation, and access to compute infrastructure. Actions focus on a scale-up accelerator, ecosystem mapping, stakeholder events, compute access, and international investor engagement.

## Key Points

- McKinsey data: 71% of global leaders see sovereign AI as an existential or strategic imperative.
- Home-grown AI companies are framed as essential for data sovereignty, skills retention, and economic resilience.
- Five 2027 actions: AI Scale-up Accelerator, national stakeholder event, national assessment of AI company needs, compute accessibility, international investor engagement via Techscaler.
- 2031 outcomes include a pipeline of companies "capable of unicorn-scale valuations."
- Case study: Gigged.AI demonstrates 30% lower hiring costs, 50% faster time-to-hire, 15% retention improvement.

## Observations

- **Clarity** -- The sovereign AI argument is clearly made and well-sourced (McKinsey). The distinction between being a "producer" and "consumer" of AI is a useful framing. However, the section does not define what sovereign AI means for Scotland specifically -- does it mean Scottish-owned models, Scottish-hosted compute, Scottish-controlled data, or all three?
- **Measurability** -- The aspiration to produce companies "capable of unicorn-scale valuations" ($1 billion+) is the strategy's most audacious outcome. Scotland has produced very few unicorns in any sector. The section provides no pathway or intermediate milestones for how this would happen. "Deliver at least one national stakeholder event" is a measurable action, but it is also the least ambitious action in the entire strategy.
- **Gaps**:
  - **No analysis of why Scottish AI companies currently struggle to scale.** The section proposes a "national assessment of AI company needs" as an action -- meaning the government doesn't yet know what the barriers are. This is honest but raises the question of whether the other actions (accelerator, investor engagement) are premature.
  - **No mention of acquisition risk.** Successful Scottish AI companies are frequently acquired by larger international firms and relocated. This is arguably the primary threat to building a sustainable AI ecosystem, and it is not addressed.
  - **Venture capital landscape is absent.** The section mentions "investment networks" but does not assess Scotland's VC ecosystem, its scale relative to London or international competitors, or what structural interventions might be needed.
  - **No connection to universities.** Spin-outs and university commercialisation are a proven pathway for AI companies (e.g. DeepMind from UCL). The section does not reference Scotland's university-to-company pipeline.
- **Feasibility** -- The gap between Scotland's current position (296 AI companies, mostly small) and the 2031 aspiration (unicorn-scale valuations, globally competitive sector) is enormous. The five actions -- a pilot accelerator, an event, a needs assessment, some compute access, and investor visibility -- are modest interventions for a transformational outcome. The strategy would benefit from acknowledging the scale of the challenge.
- **Internal consistency** -- The Gigged.AI case study is the same company quoted in the introduction (section 02). Using the same company twice suggests a thin pool of examples. The case study is strong on its own merits but demonstrates a company that helps enterprises hire talent, not a company building sovereign AI capability -- the section's central argument.
