# Review: Layer 6 - Semiconductors

## Summary

This layer focuses on Scotland's niche strengths in semiconductors, photonics, quantum, and connectivity/sensing -- collectively the "Critical Technologies supercluster." Rather than competing in mass chip fabrication, Scotland aims to lead in specialised areas: low-energy AI hardware, photonics-enabled connectivity, advanced packaging, and superconducting components. Three actions target alignment through the Critical Technologies Supercluster Advisory Board (CTSAB).

## Key Points

- Scotland's semiconductor cluster has 50+ companies with £1.2 billion annual turnover.
- The Critical Technologies supercluster spans photonics, quantum, semiconductors, and connectivity/sensing.
- Key institutions: Edinburgh's Scottish Microelectronics Centre, Glasgow's James Watt Nanofabrication Centre, NASPIC, Fraunhofer Centre for Applied Photonics.
- Niche strategy: target higher-value market segments rather than compete on volume fabrication.
- Comparator nations named: Finland, Denmark, New Zealand, Japan -- the only international benchmarks in the strategy.
- Three 2027 actions: engage CTSAB for coordination, support AI hardware spin-outs, align workforce planning with advanced technology needs.
- Case study: Quantcore -- Glasgow-based superconducting component manufacturer building sovereign supply chain capability.

## Observations

- **Clarity** -- This is one of the clearest sections in the strategy. The niche positioning is explicit and realistic -- Scotland is not trying to be Taiwan or South Korea. The focus on specialised capabilities (photonics, advanced packaging, superconducting components) rather than mass fabrication is strategically sound and well articulated.
- **Measurability** -- The £1.2 billion turnover and 50+ companies provide a baseline, which is more than most sections offer. However, the outcomes do not set growth targets. What should the turnover or company count be by 2031? How many spin-outs should the CTSAB pipeline produce? "Global success" and "leading strategic location" are not measurable.
- **Gaps**:
  - **Only three actions -- the fewest of any layer.** All three are coordination and alignment activities rather than direct interventions. No new funding, facilities, or programmes are proposed. The section relies on existing structures (CTSAB, academia, enterprise agencies) to deliver the outcomes.
  - **The UK Semiconductor Strategy is not referenced.** Semiconductors are a UK-wide strategic priority with significant UK Government investment. How Scotland's niche strategy fits within the UK strategy, and what UK-level funding Scotland can access, is not discussed.
  - **Supply chain vulnerability is acknowledged but not addressed.** The Quantcore case study highlights reliance on overseas suppliers, but the actions do not include any supply chain resilience measures.
  - **Workforce needs are vague.** Action 3 says workforce planning should "reflect the needs of Scotland's advanced technology industries" but doesn't specify what those needs are -- how many semiconductor engineers, at what level, in which specialisms?
- **Feasibility** -- The niche strategy is credible. Scotland genuinely has strengths in photonics and semiconductor design. The risk is that three coordination actions are insufficient to maintain and grow these strengths against well-funded competitors. Finland and Denmark (named as comparators) are investing heavily in their semiconductor ecosystems; the section does not indicate comparable Scottish investment.
- **Internal consistency** -- This is one of the few sections to name international comparators (Finland, Denmark, New Zealand, Japan), partially addressing the strategy-wide gap of missing international context. The Quantcore case study is well matched to the section's sovereignty argument, unlike the Eigg case study in the previous layer.
